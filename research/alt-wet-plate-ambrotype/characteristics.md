# Wet Plate Ambrotype Lightroom Preset — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## Core Aesthetic Signature

The wet plate ambrotype Lightroom preset recreates this look digitally using Lightroom's color grading tools for authentic film emulation. The ambrotype, patented by James Ambrose Cutting in 1854, is a collodion negative on glass backed with black velvet, varnish, or paint. Like the tintype, it reads as a positive against the dark backing. Ambrotypes were more delicate and expensive than tintypes but produced richer tonal range and were considered superior for portraits.

## Historical Context

**Era:** 1854–1880s

Collodion wet plate on glass. Underexposed negative bleached with mercuric chloride or nitric acid to produce a creamy white positive image. Backed with black varnish or velvet. Potassium cyanide fixing.

## Visual Characteristics

Creamy grey-white positive on deep black glass. Richer tonal gradation than tintypes — smoother transitions between highlights and shadows. The black glass backing creates incredibly deep blacks that seem to recede into the plate. Subtle warm-cool variations depending on fixing chemistry.

## Emotional & Artistic Vocabulary

Elegiac, precious, fragile. The glass substrate adds a three-dimensional depth absent from paper prints. Each image feels like a captured ghost.

## Technical Notes for Digital Emulation

This preset is designed to capture the essential visual character of the wet plate ambrotype process using Lightroom's slider-based toolset. It prioritizes the emotional/aesthetic impact over perfect chemical accuracy, which would require a 3D LUT or DCP camera profile.

### Limitations of Slider-Based Simulation
- True process-specific spectral sensitivity cannot be replicated with sliders
- Chemical stain patterns are simulated through grain and color grading
- Paper/plate texture is approximated through grain settings
