# William Eggleston

## Biography
- **Name**: William Eggleston (born 1939)
- **Nationality**: American
- **Era**: 1960s–present

## Philosophy
- Father of color art photography: legitimized color in fine art photography (1976 MoMA show)
- "I am at war with the obvious."
- "Democratic camera" — everything is worth photographing; no subject hierarchy
- Photographs the ordinary American South: tricycles, freezers, ceilings, parking lots
- Compositions that feel accidental but are obsessively precise

## Technical Approach
- **Gear**: Leica M, later 35mm SLRs, 5x7 large format, digital
- **Film**: Kodachrome, later Ektachrome, dye-transfer printing
- **Dye-transfer**: His defining print process — incredibly rich, stable color with control over individual dye layers
- **Printing**: Worked with master dye-transfer printer to achieve saturated but nuanced color

## Visual Signature
- **Ordinary subjects, extraordinary color**: A red ceiling, a blue tricycle, a green freezer — the color IS the subject
- **Slight warmth**: Not golden hour dramatic, just a subtle warm cast that makes everything feel like a summer afternoon
- **Saturated but not punchy**: Dye-transfer saturation is deep and rich, not glowing or neon
- **Overcast light preferred**: Diffuse light that lets color speak without shadow drama
- **Democratic framing**: Often centered compositions, snapshot-like but deliberate
- **No human presence**: Empty spaces populated by objects, traces of human activity

## Iconic Images
- *The Red Ceiling* (1973)
- *Untitled (Tricycle)* (1970s)
- *Freezer* (1970s)

## Preset Design Notes
- Warm color grading at low intensity — the warmth is ambient, not applied
- Saturation slightly positive to honor dye-transfer richness
- Red saturation boosted moderately (Eggleston's red)
- Green and blue pulled back for overall warmth
- Low contrast — overcast light aesthetic, no dramatic shadows
- Blending at 50 for documentary subtlety
