# Kodak Aerochrome III Infrared Film 1443 / Ektachrome EIR — Technical Datasheet

## Overview

Kodak Aerochrome III Infrared Film 1443 (consumer version: Ektachrome EIR, 35mm variant: Kodak EIR 2236) is a **false-color infrared reversal (slide) film**. Unlike true-color infrared, it produces intentionally shifted colors — the defining characteristic that makes it legendary. Discontinued circa 2009–2011.

## Emulsion Structure: 3-Layer False-Color IR

The film has three emulsion layers, each sensitive to a different spectral band:

| Layer | Native Sensitivity | False-Color Mapping (final positive) |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Cyan-forming layer** | Infrared (700–900 nm) | → Rendered as **RED** in final image |
| **Magenta-forming layer** | Red (600–700 nm) | → Rendered as **GREEN** in final image |
| **Yellow-forming layer** | Green (500–600 nm) | → Rendered as **BLUE** in final image |

### Why False Color?

- All three layers are inherently **blue-sensitive** (silver halide native sensitivity).
- A **yellow filter (#12 or #15)** or **orange filter (#16 or #22)** **must** be used over the lens to block blue light.
- Without the filter, results are unusable — blue light overwhelms all layers.
- The yellow #12 (Wratten #12, or B+W 022) is the standard. It cuts off below ~500 nm.
- B+W 040 (orange) and B+W 099 (orange) filters are also commonly used for slightly different looks.

### Spectral Sensitivity Bands

```
+-----+------------------------+
| IR  | 700–900 nm → Cyan dye  |
| Red | 600–700 nm → Magenta dye|
| Grn | 500–600 nm → Yellow dye |
+-----+------------------------+
Blue (400–500 nm) → Blocked by yellow/orange filter
```

## Filter Requirements

| Filter | Wratten # | Effect |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Yellow #12 | Wratten 12 | Standard — blocks UV/blue, passes green+red+IR |
| Yellow #15 | Wratten 15 | Deeper yellow, slightly more color separation |
| Orange #16 | Wratten 16 | Warmer tones, more dramatic magenta foliage |
| B+W 099 | — | Orange — popular among Aerochrome shooters |
| B+W 040 | — | Orange — common for cross-processed C-41 |

**Critical**: The film looks entirely white/blank without a filter. The yellow/orange filter is non-negotiable.

## Processing

### E-6 (Standard Reversal)

- Original Aerochrome III (1443) was designed for **E-6 process**.
- Earlier Ektachrome EIR used **E-4 process** (discontinued).
- E-6 produces positive slides — the canonical Aerochrome look.

### C-41 Cross-Processing

- Many community shooters cross-process in **C-41** (color negative chemistry).
- Produces a negative with even **more extreme color shifts** and increased saturation.
- Scanned negatives must be inverted — gives different color palette vs. E-6 slides.
- C-41 cross-processed Aerochrome is extremely popular on r/analog.
- C-41 processing is simpler and more accessible than E-6.

## Exposure

- **ISO 400** with Wratten #12 yellow filter (standard)
- **ISO 200** with orange filters (#16, B+W 040, B+W 099)
- **ISO 320** is a common community compromise for slightly better shadow detail
- Bracket exposures: ±1 stop recommended due to IR reflectivity unpredictability
- IR light meters read differently — TTL metering often underexposes
- **Leaf chlorophyll** reflects IR strongly → vegetation can be 2-3 stops brighter than visible light suggests
- **Water** absorbs IR → appears very dark/black in final image
- **Shadows** in IR are much darker — expect more contrast than normal color film

## Formats

- **35mm** — Kodak EIR 2236 (discontinued), sold in standard cassettes
- **120 medium format** — Aerochrome III 1443, most common format on secondary market
- **4×5 sheet film** — Aerochrome III 2443, rare
- **70mm** — Aerial mapping rolls, sometimes slit and re-spooled

## Storage & Expiration

- IR film is **more sensitive to heat and cosmic radiation** than normal film
- Must be **frozen** until use
- Expired rolls still produce images (often with color shifts) if frozen
- Load/unload in **total darkness** — felt light traps on 35mm cassettes are NOT IR-proof
- Some 35mm cameras with IR frame counters (Canon EOS, some Nikons) can fog the film

## Discontinuation

- Production officially ended **circa 2009** (35mm EIR) and **2011** (120 Aerochrome III)
- Final production run was for a U.S. government/military aerial surveillance contract
- Remaining stock on secondary market is all expired (earliest: ~2007–2011 production dates)
- No chemical equivalent currently manufactured by any company globally
- Community petition campaigns for Kodak to re-release it are frequent but unanswered
