# Agfa Scala 200 — Film Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## Overview
Agfa Scala 200 was a unique black-and-white reversal (slide) film produced by Agfa-Gevaert. Unlike most B&W films (which are negatives), Scala produced positive transparencies directly, similar to color slide film. It required a specialized reversal development process that was offered by a limited number of labs. This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates Scala 200.

## Key Characteristics
- **ISO**: 200 (nominal)
- **Type**: Black-and-white reversal (positive/slide) film
- **Grain**: Very fine — slide film character with near-invisible grain
- **Sharpness**: High — reversal process enhances edge contrast
- **Contrast**: Higher than negative films; typical slide film punch
- **D-max**: Deep, rich blacks characteristic of reversal film
- **D-min**: Clean, transparent highlights
- **Development**: Required specialized Scala reversal processing (Agfa process); effectively discontinued until a lab in Denver revived the capability

## Historical Context
- Discontinued when AgfaPhoto ceased production (2005)
- One of very few B&W slide films ever produced (alongside Fomapan R and some specialty films)
- Appealed to photographers who shot slides for projection or wanted a direct-positive B&W workflow
- The reversal process meant no negative → no darkroom printing without an internegative
- Those who loved it were devoted; those who didn't found it limiting because the reversal process offered fewer darkroom manipulation options

## Visual Signature
- Slide film character: deep blacks, clean highlights, punchy contrast
- Very smooth tonality with the brightness and "luminosity" unique to positive film
- Less grain than B&W negative films at the same speed
- Images have a luminous, projected quality when viewed directly
- The reversal process created a distinctive highlight rolloff
