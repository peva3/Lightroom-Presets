# Fuji Reala 100 — Full Aesthetic Characteristics

## Primary Character: Neutral Color Accuracy

Reala was designed and marketed for **color accuracy above all else**. Unlike Kodak's warm-biased consumer films (Gold, Ultramax) or even Portra's warm-neutral balance, Reala aimed for a **neutral white balance with true-to-life color reproduction**.

The "Reala" name itself is a portmanteau of "Real" + "Alpha" — suggesting the highest achievable realism.

## Color Palette

### Overall Rendering
- **Neutral-to-cool white balance** — No warm cast. Whites render as true white.
- **Accurate reds** — Deep, pure reds without orange shift
- **Clean greens** — Natural foliage and landscapes render with accurate green separation (thanks to 4th layer)
- **Subtle blues** — Sky and water tones are clean but not overly saturated
- **Cyan/teal accuracy** — The 4th layer's primary benefit; subtle distinctions between blues, cyans, and greens

### Skin Tones
- Widely considered **among the best of any color negative film** for skin reproduction
- Neutral, not overly warm or pink
- Excellent subtlety in lighter skin tones — avoids the "muddy" look that three-layer films can produce in shadow-side skin
- Works well for all skin tones due to its balanced spectral sensitivity

### Comparison Reference Points

| Film Stock | Comparison to Reala |
|---|---|
| **Portra 160/400** | Much warmer; Portra has a yellow-ochre bias in skin tones that Reala lacks |
| **Ektar 100** | Much more saturated; Ektar has punchy reds and high contrast |
| **Superia 400** | Close relative but coarser grain and slightly warmer |
| **Fuji Pro 400H** | Similar neutral-cool Fuji DNA but 400H has more pastel rendering |
| **Kodak Gold 200** | Dramatically warmer — Gold has a strong yellow-gold cast |
| **Pro Image 100** | The closest currently available film to Reala's neutral color neutrality per community consensus |

## Grain Structure

- **Very fine grain** for a 100-speed film (RMS ~4)
- Smooth, tight grain pattern — particularly in midtones and highlights
- Grain becomes visible but pleasant in deep shadows
- Finer grain than Superia 400, comparable to other professional 100-speed films
- In medium format (120), grain is essentially invisible at normal viewing sizes

## Contrast & Dynamic Range

- **Moderate contrast** — sitting between Portra's low contrast and Ektar's high contrast
- Good highlight retention — overexposure by +2 stops yields pleasing pastel rendering without blown highlights
- Moderate shadow density — underexposure by -1 stop is usable but shows some grain
- Performs best at box speed (100) or +1 stop overexposure (ISO 50)
- Community consensus for expired Reala: shoot at ISO 50 (1 stop over)

## Fluorescent Light Performance

This was a **signature Reala feature**. Under fluorescent lighting:

- **No green cast** — The 4th cyan-sensitive layer cancels the green spike from fluorescent tubes
- Skin tones remain natural under office/retail lighting
- White balance is more consistent across mixed lighting (fluorescent + daylight)
- Three-layer films (including all current Kodak C-41 stocks) cannot achieve this without correction filters

## Expired Film Behavior

From community experience with Reala expired 2000-2008:

- Cold-stored Reala ages gracefully — minimal color shifts when frozen/refrigerated
- Non-refrigerated Reala develops a yellow-brown cast and increased base fog
- Best results when overexposed 1 stop per decade of expiration (as with most C-41)
- Deep-frozen Reala from 1999-2000 has produced excellent results when shot at ISO 50
- Vinegar smell from poorly stored rolls indicates acetate base degradation — discard

## Overall Aesthetic Summary

Reala produces images that are:
- **Clean, crisp, and honest** — not "warm" or "vintage"
- **Exceptionally neutral** — what your eye sees is what the film captures
- **Flattering but accurate** — skin tones look natural, not enhanced
- **Ideal for** — portraits, fashion, product photography, architecture, landscapes where color accuracy matters
- **Not ideal for** — those seeking warm/vintage/"golden hour" looks (use Kodak Gold or Portra instead)

As one r/AnalogCommunity commenter put it: *"I miss four-layer color. I don't understand why everyone's so excited about 'warmth'."*
