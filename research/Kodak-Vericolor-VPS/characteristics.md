# Kodak Vericolor VPS — Film Characteristics

> ISO 160 color negative film, introduced 1979. Part of the Vericolor family. Even lower contrast than Vericolor III. "VPS" = Vericolor Print Slide. Lower contrast than Vericolor III with a late-1970s/early-80s aesthetic.

---

## Core Aesthetic Identity

| Attribute | Vericolor VPS Characteristic |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Contrast | Even lower than Vericolor III — ultra-low contrast |
| Saturation | Very pastel, subdued |
| White balance | Slightly warm-neutral (4500K warmth) |
| Skin tones | Extremely soft, low-contrast, gentle |
| Greens | Desaturated, neutralized |
| Blues | Very muted |
| Shadows | Warm brown cast (70s-80s aesthetic) |
| Highlights | Soft, blooming roll-off |
| Grain | Moderate for ISO 160 — "Strong, Large" per Fuji X Weekly |

## Historical Context

Kodak introduced Vericolor VPS in 1979 as part of the evolving Vericolor professional line. It was the predecessor to Vericolor II (1977) and Vericolor III (1983). The "VPS" designation stood for "Vericolor Print Slide" — though this is somewhat misleading, as it was a standard color negative film, not a slide film.

VPS represented Kodak's late-1970s understanding of professional portrait color science. The extremely low contrast and warm-biased palette were engineered to produce flattering results across studio flash, window light, and mixed-lighting wedding scenarios. The distinctive warm-brown shadow cast differs from Vericolor III's magenta-leaning shadows.

The film was a popular choice among wedding and portrait photographers in the late 1970s and early 1980s. It was replaced by Vericolor III, then eventually by Portra.

## Key Color Signatures

1. **Contrast**: Ultra-low — even flatter than Vericolor III. The flatness IS the look.
2. **Shadows**: Warm brown (not magenta like Vericolor III, not green, not blue).
3. **Saturation**: Very pastel, nearly desaturated in some color channels.
4. **Skin tones**: Extremely soft and forgiving — the ultimate gentle portrait rendering.
5. **Whites**: Roll off with a warm glow, never clip.
6. **Blacks**: Very lifted — deep blacks don't exist in VPS.
7. **Grain**: More prominent than Vericolor III — "Strong, Large" for the period look.

## Fuji X Weekly Recipe (2025)

The [Fuji X Weekly recipe by Ritchie Roesch](https://fujixweekly.com/2025/07/07/) notes that despite being named after VPS (1979), the recipe actually resembles Vericolor II (1977) more closely. WB 4500K with +2 Red and -5 Blue creates the slight warmth. Strong/Large grain and Shadow +2 contribute to the vintage character.

## Vericolor VPS vs Vericolor III 160

| Characteristic | VPS (1979) | Vericolor III (1983) |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Contrast | Ultra-low | Very low |
| Shadow cast | Warm brown | Cool magenta |
| Color temperature | Slightly warm | Cool-neutral |
| Grain | Strong, large | Weak, small |
| Era aesthetic | Late 1970s | Mid-late 1980s |
| Best for | Vintage wedding, nostalgic | School portrait, commercial |

## Relevance for Digital Emulation

Vericolor VPS is the most contrast-flattened preset in the collection. Its -40 contrast and +25 blacks create the most extreme "pastel wash" of any Color-Negative preset. The warm shadow cast (Hue 30°) differentiates it from Vericolor III's magenta shadows.
