### FxW Recipe → Lightroom Translation

HD 200 was Kodak's sharpest consumer film — T-Grain technology for maximum resolution. Translation priorities: neutral-warm color (less golden than Gold 200), medium contrast, ultra-fine grain, clean rendering.

#### Basic Panel
- **Exposure**: +0.15 (consumer film baseline)
- **Contrast**: +15 (medium — less than Gold 200, let resolution carry texture)
- **Highlights**: -28 (controlled T-Grain highlight handling)
- **Shadows**: +18 (open shadows — T-Grain shadow detail benefit)
- **Whites**: 0 (neutral white point)
- **Blacks**: -12 (gentle black point — fine grain in shadows)

#### Color Grading
- **Highlights**: Hue 45°, Sat 8 (subtle warmth, less than Gold)
- **Shadows**: Hue 210°, Sat 5 (very subtle cool shadow)
- **Blending**: 50
- **Balance**: +10 (slight warm bias)

#### Grain (Fine-grain consumer: Amount 15-25, Size 20-25, Roughness 40-55)
- **Amount**: 18 (very fine — T-Grain advantage)
- **Size**: 22 (small particles)
- **Roughness**: 45 (regular, uniform)

#### STYLEGUIDE Compliance
- No Calibration
- No Temperature/Tint
- Sharpness=10, Clarity=0, Texture=0, Dehaze=0 (grain protection)
- LuminanceSmoothing=0
- Vibrance 0, Saturation 0 (gap = 0)
- Blacks -12 ≥ -30

## Community Consensus

HD 200 is noted by expired film shooters and nostalgia photographers for:
1. **Sharpness above all**: The name wasn't marketing — HD 200 genuinely resolved more detail than any consumer color film
2. **Neutral color**: Less golden than Gold 200, more natural palette
3. **T-Grain smoothness**: Grain structure more like professional films than consumer
4. **Forgotten gem**: Overshadowed by Gold 200's ubiquity but objectively superior
5. **Landscape film**: Excellent for outdoor/detail work where Gold 200's warmth was unwanted
