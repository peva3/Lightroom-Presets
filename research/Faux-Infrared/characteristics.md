# Faux Infrared Lightroom Preset — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## The Core Look

The Faux Infrared Lightroom preset recreates this look digitally using Lightroom's color grading tools for authentic film emulation. Faux infrared photography reproduces the surreal, otherworldly appearance of infrared film using digital post-processing. The aesthetic is defined by **three signature visual elements**: glowing white foliage, dramatically dark skies, and an uncanny separation between organic and inorganic subjects.

---

## 1. Glowing White Foliage (The Wood Effect)

### The Signature
The defining characteristic of infrared photography. Healthy vegetation — leaves, grass, crops, moss — appears **luminous white or near-white**, as if covered in frost or illuminated from within. This is the digital recreation of the **Wood Effect**.

### Visual Properties
- **Luminance dominance:** Foliage is the brightest element in the frame, often clipping to pure white.
- **Texture retention:** Despite brightness, leaf texture (veins, edges) remains visible — a balance between +Luminance and -Highlights recovery.
- **Differential response:** Deciduous trees (broad leaves) glow more than conifers (needles). Healthy plants glow more than stressed/dying ones.
- **Seasonal dependency:** Spring/summer foliage reflects maximum IR; autumn leaves and bare branches do not. This mirrors real IR film behavior.
- **Color temperature shift:** In color faux IR, foliage shifts to **pink/magenta/crimson** (Aerochrome) or stark white (B&W IR).

### Aesthetic Effect
The glowing foliage creates a **dreamlike, ethereal quality**. Landscapes appear transformed into alien worlds. The effect is simultaneously beautiful and unsettling — familiar nature rendered unfamiliar. This tension between the real and the surreal is central to the aesthetic's power.

---

## 2. Dark, Dramatic Skies

### The Signature
Skies in infrared photography appear **abnormally dark — near-black or deep charcoal** — even on bright sunny days. This is because the atmosphere scatters very little infrared radiation (reduced Rayleigh scattering at longer wavelengths).

### Visual Properties
- **Blue sky → near black:** The deeper the blue in visible light, the darker in IR.
- **Clouds remain bright white:** Water droplets in clouds scatter IR efficiently → maximum contrast between sky and clouds.
- **Dramatic cloud definition:** Every cloud formation becomes dramatic and sculptural against the dark backdrop.
- **No haze:** Atmospheric haze (Mie scattering) is significantly reduced at IR wavelengths → distant objects appear sharper and closer.
- **Gradient from horizon:** The sky darkens most at the zenith, lightening slightly toward the horizon (where more atmosphere = more scatter).

### Aesthetic Effect
The dark sky + white cloud combination creates a **storm-like, apocalyptic atmosphere** even in fair weather. Images gain a sense of impending drama. This is the most immediately recognizable IR characteristic and the easiest to simulate.

---

## 3. Surreal Landscape Transformation

### Foliage-Sky Separation
The combination of white foliage + dark sky creates **extreme subject-background separation** impossible in visible-light photography. Trees and plants seem to jump out of the frame against the black sky.

### Water Behavior
- **Water absorbs IR strongly** → lakes, rivers, and oceans appear **jet black or very dark**.
- Reflections are subdued or eliminated.
- Waterfalls and white water remain bright (foam scatters IR).
- Wet surfaces (rocks, soil) appear darker than dry ones.

### Shadow Behavior
- Shadows are **deeper and more opaque** than in visible light.
- Less IR light scatters into shadow areas from the sky.
- Shadow details can be lost completely → high contrast aesthetic.
- This contributes to the **graphic, high-impact visual style**.

### Human Subjects
- **Skin becomes milky, porcelain-like** — IR penetrates a few millimeters into skin, smoothing blemishes.
- **Eyes appear black** — the iris and pupil absorb IR.
- **Clothing can shift unpredictably** — synthetic fabrics may reflect or absorb IR differently than they appear in visible light.

### Urban and Man-Made Elements
- Buildings, roads, and concrete typically appear **darker and more neutral** — they don't reflect IR as strongly as vegetation.
- This creates a **nature-vs-civilization contrast**: organic subjects glow, inorganic subjects recede.
- In Aerochrome color simulation, urban scenes take on an eerie quality — pink trees against dark buildings.

## 4. Color Palette (False-Color / Aerochrome Simulation)

### Primary Palette
| Element | Visible Color | Color Faux IR |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| Healthy foliage (deciduous) | Green | **Magenta / Crimson / Hot Pink** |
| Healthy foliage (grass) | Green | **Red-Pink** |
| Conifers | Dark green | **Muted purple-brown** |
| Sky | Blue | **Near-black / Deep navy** |
| Clouds | White | **White / Slightly warm** |
| Water | Blue-green | **Black / Very dark teal** |
| Soil / earth | Brown | **Dark brown / Grey** |
| Skin | Varies | **Milky pale / Yellow-tinted** |
| Dead/dry vegetation | Brown/yellow | **Dark / Muted** |

### Emotional Associations
- **Magenta/crimson foliage** → alien, psychedelic, unsettling beauty
- **Dark skies** → ominous, dramatic, sublime
- **Luminous whites** → ethereal, spiritual, transcendent
- **High contrast** → graphic, bold, confrontational
- The overall palette evokes **1960s psychedelia** (Jimi Hendrix, Donovan, Grateful Dead all used IR film for album covers).

## 5. B&W vs. Color Faux IR — Different Aesthetics

### Black & White Infrared
- **Timeless, classic, fine-art** aesthetic.
- Emphasizes **texture, form, and light** over color.
- Closer to traditional B&W landscape photography but with heightened drama.
- The Wood Effect is the sole focus — glowing white foliage against dark everything.
- Less "gimmicky" feel; more acceptable in fine-art contexts.
- References: Ansel Adams (experimented with IR), Minor White, contemporary landscape photographers.

### Color / False-Color Infrared
- **Surreal, psychedelic, provocative.**
- Color becomes a **political/emotional statement** (see Richard Mosse).
- Unmistakably altered reality — no pretense of naturalism.
- Can overwhelm the subject if not used intentionally.
- References: Richard Mosse, 1960s album covers, contemporary art photography.

## 7. Technical Constraints of the Simulation

### What Faux IR Cannot Replicate
- **True IR reflectance data:** Digital sensors with hot mirrors block IR. Faux IR is a color/luminance remapping, not actual IR capture.
- **Material differentiation:** Real IR distinguishes materials invisible to the eye (camouflage detection, species identification). Faux IR cannot.
- **IR penetration of haze:** Faux IR can simulate the look but cannot actually see through haze.
- **Fluorescence effects:** Real IR captures chlorophyll fluorescence (minor). Faux IR cannot.

### What Faux IR Excels At
- The **emotional/aesthetic impact** is identical to real IR film.
- Greater control — luminance per channel, tone curves, calibration.
- No film cost, no special equipment, no processing chemistry.
- Works with any digital photo (best with RAW files for latitude).

## 8. Compositional Best Practices for Faux IR

### Subject Selection
1. **Maximize foliage in frame** — the more vegetation, the stronger the IR effect.
2. **Include sky** — the dark sky is essential contrast for white foliage.
3. **Include water** — dark water bodies add depth and variety.
4. **Shoot in full sun** — maximum IR reflectance from leaves; flat/overcast light yields muted results.
5. **Avoid large areas of bare earth, rock, pavement** — these go dark and muddy.
6. **Solitary trees against sky** — classic IR composition; maximum impact.

### Light Direction
- **Front-lit foliage** reflects maximum IR toward the camera.
- **Back-lit foliage** creates glowing halos around leaves.
- **Side-lit** accentuates texture and form.
- **Midday sun** (harshest visible light) produces the strongest IR reflectance.
