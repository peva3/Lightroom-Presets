# Film References — Aerochrome, Infrared History & Key Artists

## Kodak Aerochrome / Ektachrome EIR

### Overview
Kodak Aerochrome is a false-color infrared reversal (slide) film originally developed during **World War II for military reconnaissance and camouflage detection**. Healthy vegetation reflects infrared strongly; green paint (used for camouflage) does not. Aerochrome made camouflaged positions immediately visible. After the war, it was adopted by the scientific community (aerial vegetation surveys, forestry, crop analysis) and, beginning in the 1960s, released to consumers as **Kodak Ektachrome Infrared (EIR)**.

### Key Specifications
- **Film type:** Color reversal (slide) film, E-6 compatible (optimally AR-5).
- **Format:** 35mm (EIR code 2236), 120/medium format, 4×5 sheets, 70mm bulk (Aerochrome codes 1443, SO-734).
- **Spectral sensitivity:** Infrared (~700–900 nm), visible red, visible green. Visible blue blocked by external yellow Wratten #12 filter.
- **False-color mapping:** IR → red, Red → green, Green → blue.
- **Base:** Clear polyester (must be loaded in total darkness).
- **Processing:** E-6 (E-4 originally; AR-5 for optimal/accurate color).
- **Discontinued:** All formats discontinued by 2011.
- **Current availability:** Only expired stock. Hand-rolled by specialists; ~$100+ per 35mm roll.

### Notable Uses
- **Military reconnaissance (1940s–present):** Camouflage detection.
- **Aerial vegetation surveys:** Forestry, agriculture, environmental monitoring.
- **1960s album covers:** Jimi Hendrix *Are You Experienced* (1967), Donovan, Frank Zappa, Grateful Dead.
- **Richard Mosse:** *Infra* (2012), *The Enclave* (2013) — documenting conflict in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo.
- **Contemporary fine art:** Multiple international artists working with expired Aerochrome stock.

### Visual Signature
- **Foliage:** Vivid magenta/crimson/hot pink (the stronger the IR reflectance, the redder).
- **Sky:** Deep navy to near-black (when yellow filter used).
- **Clouds:** Brilliant white against dark sky.
- **Water:** Very dark to black.
- **Soil/earth:** Muted brown-grey.
- **Conifers:** Purple-brown (lower IR reflectance than broadleaf trees).

## History of Infrared Photography

### Early Pioneers (1910–1930s)
- **Robert W. Wood (1910):** Published first infrared photographs in *The Century Magazine* (Feb 1910) and *Royal Photographic Society Journal* (Oct 1910). Discovered the "Wood Effect" — foliage appearing white in IR. His early work required very long exposures on experimental plates.
- **C.E.K. Mees (1910):** Took IR photographs in Portugal; worked at Wratten & Wainwright (later Kodak). His plates are in the Kodak archives.
- **World War I:** IR-sensitive plates developed in the US for spectroscopic analysis and aerial haze penetration.

### Commercial Era (1930s–1960s)
- **1930s:** IR film becomes commercially available. By 1937, 33 types of IR film from 5 manufacturers (Kodak, Agfa, Ilford, etc.).
- **The Times (London):** Regularly published IR landscape and aerial photographs.
- **Motion pictures:** IR film used for day-for-night effects (e.g., *The Bride Came C.O.D.* with James Cagney and Bette Davis).
- **1940s:** Kodak develops false-color IR film for camouflage detection (becomes Aerochrome).

### The Psychedelic Era (1960s–1970s)
- IR photography explodes in popular culture alongside psychedelic art.
- **Jimi Hendrix - *Are You Experienced* (1967):** Fish-eye lens portrait shot on color IR film. Iconic album cover.
- **Donovan, Frank Zappa, Grateful Dead:** All used IR film for album artwork.
- The unexpected colors fit perfectly with the psychedelic aesthetic — IR became associated with altered perception and expanded consciousness.
- Kodak releases EIR to consumers in the 1960s.

### Decline and Discontinuation (1990s–2011)
- Digital photography reduces demand for niche film stocks.
- **Konica Infrared 750** discontinued in 2006.
- **Kodak HIE (High-Speed Infrared)** discontinued at end of 2007.
- **Kodak EIR / Aerochrome:** 35mm version discontinued 2007; all formats discontinued by 2011.
- **Efke IR820** discontinued in 2012.

### Contemporary Revival (2010s–Present)
- **Dean Bennici:** LA-based photographer who began cutting and hand-rolling expired Aerochrome stock in 2008. Most existing Aerochrome medium/large format passed through his lab.
- **Richard Mosse's success** (Deutsche Börse Prize 2014, Prix Pictet 2017) brought massive renewed interest to Aerochrome.
- **Digital simulation:** Faux IR presets, Photoshop channel swapping, and Lightroom techniques have made the aesthetic accessible to all photographers.
- **Converted digital cameras:** Some photographers remove the hot mirror from digital sensors and use IR-passing filters for genuine digital IR capture.

## Richard Mosse — The Definitive Aerochrome Artist

### Biography
- **Born:** 1980, Kilkenny, Ireland.
- **Education:** King's College London (BA English), London Consortium (MRes), Goldsmiths (PGDip Fine Art), Yale School of Art (MFA Photography, 2008).
- **Based in:** New York City and Ireland.
- **Website:** richardmosse.com

### *Infra* (2010–2012)
- **Medium:** Still photography on Kodak Aerochrome infrared film.
- **Subject:** The ongoing conflict in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo.
- **Intent:** To create "a new perspective on conflict" — making the familiar seem strange, the real seem heightened to the point of absurdity.
- **Visual language:** Rebel soldiers in pink/magenta uniforms against crimson jungles. The surreal palette disrupts conventional war photography tropes.
- **Publication:** *Infra*, Aperture 2012, with essay by Adam Hochschild.
- **Reception:** "This is war reportage — but not as we know it." — Sean O'Hagan, *The Guardian*.

### *The Enclave* (2013)
- **Medium:** Six-screen video installation, shot on 16mm infrared film transferred to HD.
- **Collaborators:** Cinematographer Trevor Tweeten, composer Ben Frost.
- **Commission:** National Pavilion of Ireland, 55th Venice Biennale.
- **Subject:** Eastern Congo conflict, presented as an immersive multi-screen experience in a darkened chamber.
- **Awards:** **Deutsche Börse Photography Prize 2014**.
- **Significance:** Brought Aerochrome into the contemporary art mainstream. The work's success is credited with the renewed global interest in infrared film aesthetics.

### *Incoming* (2017)
- **Medium:** Video installation using military-grade thermal imaging (far-infrared, not near-IR Aerochrome).
- **Subject:** The European migrant/refugee crisis.
- **Collaborators:** Ben Frost, Trevor Tweeten.
- **Commission:** Barbican Art Gallery and National Gallery of Victoria.
- **Awards:** **Prix Pictet 2017** (*Heat Maps*).
- **Note:** Represents Mosse's shift from near-IR Aerochrome to far-IR thermal imaging — a technological and conceptual evolution.

### *Broken Spectre* (2022)
- **Medium:** 74-minute video installation on a 20-meter panoramic screen.
- **Subject:** Deforestation of the Amazon rainforest.
- **Collaborators:** Ben Frost, Trevor Tweeten.
- **Commission:** National Gallery of Victoria, SFMOMA, Serpentine Galleries.
- **Exhibited:** NGV Melbourne, Minnesota Street Project/SFMOMA, PHI Centre Montreal, Madison MOCA.

### Critical Reception
- **Praise:** Acclaimed for using beauty and aesthetics as a tool to draw attention to overlooked humanitarian and environmental crises. "Beauty is the sharpest tool in the box" — Mosse.
- **Criticism:** Some critics have described the work as "troubling" and "discomforting" — questioning whether highly aestheticized presentations of conflict and suffering are appropriate. Mosse's defenders argue the work's nuanced meaning is oversimplified by such critiques.
- **Influence:** Mosse's work with Aerochrome has inspired a generation of photographers to explore infrared aesthetics, both with expired film and digital simulation.

## Other Notable Infrared Photographers and Artists

### Historical
- **Robert W. Wood (1868–1955):** American physicist, inventor of the Wood Effect. First published IR photographs, 1910.
- **Minor White (1908–1976):** American photographer who experimented with infrared film for fine-art landscape work.
- **Ansel Adams (1902–1984):** Experimented with infrared film, though most famous for his visible-light zone system work.

### Contemporary Film Users
- **Dean Bennici:** LA-based; primary source for hand-rolled Aerochrome since 2008.
- **Various international artists:** Working across landscape, portraiture, and conceptual genres with expired IR film stock.

### Digital Artists (Faux IR)
- Countless photographers on Instagram, Flickr, 500px using faux infrared presets and techniques.
- The digital simulation community has democratized the aesthetic beyond the constraints of expired film stock.

## Infrared Film in Popular Culture

### Music Album Covers
| Artist | Album | Year | Notes |
|--------|-------|------|-------|
| Jimi Hendrix Experience | *Are You Experienced* | 1967 | Fish-eye + Aerochrome. Most famous IR album cover. |
| Donovan | *A Gift from a Flower to a Garden* | 1967 | IR photography on cover/inserts |
| Frank Zappa | *Hot Rats* | 1969 | IR-influenced color palette (though not confirmed pure IR film) |
| Grateful Dead | *Anthem of the Sun* | 1968 | IR imagery used in cover art |

### Film and Cinema
- **Day-for-night effects:** IR film used in 1930s–1940s Hollywood to shoot daytime scenes that appear as night (dark skies, glowing foliage readable as moonlight).
- ***The Bride Came C.O.D.*** (1941): Notable example of IR day-for-night cinematography.
- Contemporary documentaries and art films occasionally use IR for surreal/psychedelic sequences.
