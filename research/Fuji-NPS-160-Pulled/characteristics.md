# Fuji NPS 160 Pulled — Film Characteristics

> Fujicolor NPS 160 pulled 1 stop (overexposed + reduced development). Low contrast, low saturation, cooler color balance, finer grain, soft contrast portrait film. Pulled processing further reduces contrast and saturation for an ethereal portrait aesthetic.

---

## Core Aesthetic Identity

| Attribute | NPS 160 Pulled Characteristic |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Contrast | Very low — further reduced by pull processing |
| Saturation | Very low — desaturated, almost pastel |
| White balance | Cool with green-cyan shadow bias |
| Skin tones | Soft, de-saturated, gentle |
| Greens | Desaturated, slight cyan shift |
| Blues | Muted, cool |
| Shadows | Green-cyan cast (Classic Negative signature) |
| Highlights | Soft, diffused |
| Grain | Very fine (ISO 160, pull process further reduces grain) |

## Historical Context

Fujicolor NPS 160 was introduced in the 1990s as Fuji's professional portrait film (NPS = "Nikon Professional System" was a common misconception — the designation was simply Fuji's naming convention for portrait films). It was a direct competitor to Kodak's Vericolor III line, designed for studio portraiture, weddings, and commercial work.

NPS 160 was replaced in 2004 by Fujicolor Pro 160S (later renamed Pro 160NS), which incorporated Fuji's 4th color layer technology.

**Pulling film** (overexposing and reducing development time) further reduces contrast and saturation — the opposite of push processing. The result is an even softer, more ethereal rendering with finer grain and extended highlight latitude. Photographers pulled NPS 160 for bridal portraits, high-key fashion, and dreamy editorial work.

## Key Color Signatures

1. **Contrast**: Very low — deliberately flattened via pull processing
2. **Saturation**: Very desaturated — colors are muted and gentle
3. **Shadows**: Green-cyan cast (characteristic of Classic Negative base on Fuji digital)
4. **Skin tones**: Soft, de-saturated, never harsh
5. **Highlights**: Extended latitude from overexposure + reduced development
6. **Blacks**: Lifted, never crushed — pull processing opens up shadows
7. **Grain**: Extremely fine — pull processing reduces effective grain visibility

## Fuji X Weekly Recipe (2021)

The [recipe by Thomas Schwab](https://fujixweekly.com/2021/05/30/) uses Classic Negative as the base film simulation, which provides the green-shadow bias and muted color palette. Strong Color Chrome Effect Blue deepens the cool tones. The combination of Classic Negative + DR400 + Highlight -2 + Shadow -2 + Color -4 creates the pulled, low-contrast aesthetic.
