## Integral Film Chemistry

### How Polaroid Integral Film Works

The SX-70 introduced **integral film** — the first instant film that developed automatically without requiring the photographer to peel apart layers. This was Edwin Land's breakthrough solution.

#### Layer Stack (SX-70 Integral Film — Same-Side View/Expose)

From top (viewer side) to bottom:

1. **Clear polyester layer** — protective top sheet
2. **Acid polymer layer** — neutralises the alkaline reagent after development
3. **Timing layer** — controls development duration
4. **Image-receiving (mordant) layer** — captures and fixes migrating dyes
5. **Reagent layer** — white titania pigment + opacifying dyes + potassium hydroxide + thickener
6. **Cyan dye developer layer** — releases cyan dye where silver remains undeveloped
7. **Red-sensitive silver halide emulsion** — exposed by red light
8. **Interlayer**
9. **Magenta dye developer layer**
10. **Green-sensitive silver halide emulsion**
11. **Interlayer**
12. **Yellow dye developer layer**
13. **Blue-sensitive silver halide emulsion**
14. **Anti-abrasion layer**
15. **Opaque polyester back** — black backing

#### Development Process

1. **Exposure**: Light enters through the lens, hits the front (clear polyester side), passes through all layers to the emulsion stack
2. **Ejection**: Motor-driven rollers press a reagent pod at the bottom edge, breaking it and spreading the alkaline reagent evenly between the receiving layer and the dye-developer stack
3. **Opacification**: The opacifying dyes (opaque at high pH) darken immediately upon spreading, protecting the image from further light exposure while it develops
4. **Development**: The alkaline reagent (KOH) solubilises the dye developers. Exposed silver halide is reduced to metallic silver (the latent image). Unexposed silver halide releases silver ions that control dye mobility
5. **Dye diffusion**: Dye molecules migrate upward through the reagent by diffusion, driven by electrostatic and chemical gradients, depositing in the mordant/receiving layer
6. **Neutralisation**: As the acid polymer layer slowly neutralises the alkaline reagent, the opacifying dyes become clear at lower pH, revealing the final image
7. **Complete**: Full image visible in ~10 minutes (original Time-Zero); ~15 minutes (modern Polaroid B.V. remakes)

#### Key Chemistry Characteristics

- **Dye developers** are metal-complex dyes: copper (cyan), chromium (magenta, yellow) — giving subtle metallic undertones
- **Gelatin-based emulsion** stays soft for hours/days after development, enabling the famous "emulsion manipulation" art technique
- **Reagent spread** is never perfectly uniform, contributing to the characteristic uneven edges and chemical artifacts
- **Temperature sensitivity**: Development rate is highly temperature-dependent; cold = slow/blue shift, hot = fast/yellow-red shift

## SX-70 Film Generations

### Original Polaroid Corp. Films

| Film | Era | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Original SX-70 (1972)** | 1972–1975 | First integral film; slower development; softer contrast |
| **New Improved Faster Developing** | ~1975–1979 | Mid-generation refinement |
| **Time-Zero Supercolor (1980)** | 1980–2005 | Altered layer structure for much faster development; richer, brighter, higher-saturation colours; the definitive classic SX-70 look |
| **Type 778 (Time-Zero equivalent)** | Professional | Same as Time-Zero but without battery (for lab/pro backs) |
| **Type 708** | Professional | Time-Zero without battery |

### Modern Polaroid B.V. Films (Impossible Project / Polaroid Originals)

| Film | Era | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **PX 70 Color Shade (First Flush)** | 2010 | Experimental colour system; unstable, pastel-shifted |
| **PX 70 Color Shade Push!** | 2010 | Ongoing chemical reaction; shifts to blue over time |
| **PX 70 Color Shade / Cool** | 2011–2012 | Progressively refined colour formula |
| **PX 70 Color Protection** | ~2013 | Introduction of UV+ protection layer; better colour stability |
| **Color SX-70 Film** | 2013–present | Current standard; ISO 160; 8 exposures per pack |

Modern films use different chemistry than original Polaroid:
- **Lower colour accuracy** — modern films have a distinct colour cast (often warm/magenta-green shifted)
- **Longer development times** — ~15 minutes vs original ~10 minutes
- **Higher sensitivity** to light during first seconds after ejection (must be shielded)
- **Greater temperature sensitivity**
- **Lithium-ion battery** instead of original zinc-chloride Polapulse

## Comparison: SX-70 vs 600 Film

| Property | SX-70 | 600 |
|---|---|---|
| **ISO** | 150/160 | 640 |
| **Speed difference** | 2 stops slower than 600 | 2 stops faster than SX-70 |
| **Image area** | Same (79 × 79 mm) | Same (79 × 79 mm) |
| **Cartridge** | Same physical format | Same physical format |
| **Battery** | 6V Polapulse (film pack) | 6V Polapulse (film pack) |
| **Native camera compatibility** | SX-70 cameras only | 600 cameras only |
| **Cross-compatibility** | ND filter or circuit mod needed to use 600 film in SX-70 | Cannot use SX-70 film in 600 cameras without mod |

---

## 600 Film Specifics (for cross-reference)

Introduced in 1981, the 600 series film used the same cartridge format as SX-70 but at ISO 640 (2 stops faster). The chemistry was reformulated for faster emulsions. The 600 look is generally **more saturated, higher contrast, and cooler in white balance** than the classic SX-70 Time-Zero warmth. Modern Polaroid B.V. produces both SX-70 and 600 film, with 600 being ISO 640.
