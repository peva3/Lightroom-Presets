# Ansel Adams Zone System

## Biography
- **Name**: Ansel Easton Adams (1902–1984)
- **Nationality**: American
- **Era**: 1920s–1980s

## Philosophy
- Co-created the Zone System with Fred Archer in 1939–1940
- 11-zone tonal scale from pure black (Zone 0) to pure white (Zone X)
- "You don't take a photograph, you make it" — previsualization was central
- F/64 Group member — sharp focus, maximum depth of field, pure photographic technique
- Environmental advocacy through photography (Sierra Club)

## Technical Approach
- **Gear**: Large format cameras (8x10, 4x5), Zeiss and Schneider lenses
- **Film**: Kodak Super-XX, Tri-X, Panatomic-X; later Polaroid Type 55
- **Filters**: Heavy use of red (#25) and yellow (#8, #12) filters for sky separation
- **Processing**: Water bath development, selenium toning for archival permanence
- **Printing**: Exquisite darkroom skill, burning and dodging, variable contrast papers

## Visual Signature
- **Red filter skies**: Dramatically darkened blue skies that separate clouds and mountain peaks. Blue channel pulled to near-black in the gray conversion.
- **Full tonal range**: Intentional placement of shadows, midtones, and highlights across all 11 zones. No crushed blacks, no clipped whites — everything deliberate.
- **Sharp everywhere**: f/64 aesthetic means everything from foreground to infinity is tack-sharp.
- **High microcontrast**: Texture in rock faces, tree bark, and clouds was a defining characteristic.
- **Luminous snow/water**: Zone VIII–IX placement gave snow and water a radiant glow without clipping.

## Iconic Images
- *Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico* (1941)
- *Clearing Winter Storm, Yosemite* (1937)
- *The Tetons and the Snake River* (1942)

## Preset Design Notes
- Red filter simulation via B&W mixer (Blue -60, Red +40)
- Deep blacks but not crushed (Zone 0–I preserved)
- Strong contrast with broad midrange to show all 11 zones
- Subtle grain (large format had very fine grain)
