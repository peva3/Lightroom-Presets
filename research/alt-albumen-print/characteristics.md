# Albumen Print — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## Core Aesthetic Signature

The albumen print, introduced by Louis Désiré Blanquart-Evrard in 1850, was the dominant photographic print process from 1855–1890. Egg white (albumen) was used as a binder layer to hold the light-sensitive silver salts on the paper surface, creating a glossy surface and preventing the image from sinking into paper fibers. The characteristic warm yellow-brown tone comes from both the albumen layer itself and sulfur-based toning.

## Historical Context

**Era:** 1850–1890s (dominant 1855–1890)

Paper coated with salted egg white (albumen + ammonium/sodium chloride) → sensitized with silver nitrate → contact printed under negative in sunlight → gold chloride toned → fixed with sodium thiosulfate. The albumen layer yellows with age, deepening the characteristic warm tone.

## Visual Characteristics

Warm yellow-brown to purple-brown image on cream/yellow paper base. Glossy surface from the albumen coating. Relatively high contrast with bright paper highlights. The paper base is distinctly yellow-warm, not white. Shadows tend toward warm brown-black. Characteristic albumen 'gloss' that catches light directionally.

## Emotional & Artistic Vocabulary

Victorian, scientific, classic. The warm yellow-brown palette immediately evokes 19th-century photography — cartes-de-visite, stereoviews, and the great survey photographers (Timothy O'Sullivan, Carleton Watkins).

## Technical Notes for Digital Emulation

This preset is designed to capture the essential visual character of the albumen print process using Lightroom's slider-based toolset. It prioritizes the emotional/aesthetic impact over perfect chemical accuracy, which would require a 3D LUT or DCP camera profile.

### Limitations of Slider-Based Simulation
- True process-specific spectral sensitivity cannot be replicated with sliders
- Chemical stain patterns are simulated through grain and color grading
- Paper/plate texture is approximated through grain settings
