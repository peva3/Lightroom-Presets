# Autochrome — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## Core Aesthetic Signature

The Autochrome Lumière, patented in 1903 by the Lumière brothers, was the first commercially successful color photography process. It used a glass plate coated with microscopic potato starch grains dyed red-orange, green, and blue-violet, overlaid with a panchromatic silver halide emulsion. Light passed through the starch grains (acting as color filters), exposed the emulsion, and was reversal-processed to create a positive transparency with a distinctive pointillist color palette.

## Historical Context

**Era:** 1907–1935 (peak 1910–1920)

Dyed potato starch grains (red-orange, green, blue-violet) → lampblack fills gaps → panchromatic emulsion → exposure through the plate back (through starch filters) → reversal processing → positive. The starch grains act as both color separation filters and viewing filters.

## Visual Characteristics

Pointillist color rendering — colors are composed of tiny dots rather than continuous tone. Soft, pastel palette — autochromes cannot render saturated, vivid colors. Warm color bias overall. Noticeable grain structure from the starch grains. Slightly soft focus from light scattering through grain layer. Dreamlike, nostalgic color rendering.

## Emotional & Artistic Vocabulary

Dreamy, nostalgic, precious. Autochromes capture a world that seems already remembered rather than presently seen. The pastel pointillism makes everything feel like a fading memory.

## Technical Notes for Digital Emulation

This preset is designed to capture the essential visual character of the autochrome process using Lightroom's slider-based toolset. It prioritizes the emotional/aesthetic impact over perfect chemical accuracy, which would require a 3D LUT or DCP camera profile.

### Limitations of Slider-Based Simulation
- True process-specific spectral sensitivity cannot be replicated with sliders
- Chemical stain patterns are simulated through grain and color grading
- Paper/plate texture is approximated through grain settings
