# Film References — Cinematography Breakdowns

## Overview

Detailed cinematography breakdowns of the three primary films for the Wes Anderson Pastel aesthetic: *The Grand Budapest Hotel* (2014), *Moonrise Kingdom* (2012), and *Asteroid City* (2023). All three were shot by DP **Robert Yeoman, ASC**, Anderson's longtime cinematographer.

---

## The Grand Budapest Hotel (2014)

### Cinematography Details

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Director of Photography | Robert Yeoman, ASC |
| Camera | Arricam LT, Arricam ST |
| Lenses | Zeiss Master Primes, Cooke S4 |
| Film Stock | Kodak Vision3 200T (5213), 500T (5219) |
| Format | 35mm, 3-perf |
| Aspect Ratios | 1.37:1 (Academy), 1.85:1 (flat), 2.35:1 (anamorphic) |
| DI / Color | EFilm, colorist Jill Bogdanowicz |
| Year | 2014 |

### Visual Strategy

*The Grand Budapest Hotel* uses three aspect ratios to separate the three time periods of the story:

- **1.37:1 Academy Ratio (1930s)**: The bulk of the film. The nearly-square frame heightens the verticality of the hotel and creates a "classical Hollywood" feel. This ratio works perfectly with Anderson's symmetrical compositions.
- **1.85:1 (1960s)**: The "Author" sequences with Jude Law. The wider frame signals a more contemporary, less fantastical era.
- **2.35:1 Anamorphic (1980s)**: The framing device with the girl reading at the monument. The widest ratio for the most "modern" time period.

### Color Strategy by Time Period

#### 1930s — Oversaturated Storybook
- Most vibrant color palette of Anderson's career.
- Extensive use of pink (the hotel facade, Mendl's boxes).
- Purple (Gustave's uniforms).
- Deep red (interiors, elevator).
- Gold trim throughout sets.
- Pastel blue skies in exteriors.
- Color is deliberately artificial — the world looks like a confection.

#### 1960s — Faded Autumn
- Warmer, more muted palette.
- Mustard yellows, faded oranges.
- The hotel is "past its prime" — colors reflect decay.
- Dust collected on once-bright surfaces.

#### 1980s — Cold, Desaturated
- Near-monochromatic teal/gray.
- Minimal warmth.
- The modern world has no color — only the book and the story retain it.

### Lighting Approach
- Large, soft sources throughout.
- The hotel lobby was built as a practical set with large windows allowing naturalistic daylight.
- Interior practical lamps glow warm amber (2700-3200K).
- Low contrast ratio: shadows are filled heavily.
- Almost no hard light or strong directional shadows.

### Production Design Impact on Color
- Sets were painted in specific colors — the color grade enhances rather than creates the palette.
- The iconic pink of the hotel facade was practical paint, not a digital grade.
- Mendl's boxes were physically crafted in the exact pink-blue combination.
- This means the color grade was a *collaboration* between production design and post, not purely a post-production effect.

## Moonrise Kingdom (2012)

### Cinematography Details

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Director of Photography | Robert Yeoman, ASC |
| Camera | Aaton XTR Prod |
| Lenses | Zeiss Super Speed MkII, Canon zooms |
| Film Stock | Kodak Vision3 200T (7213) |
| Format | Super 16mm, 1.85:1 extraction |
| DI / Color | EFilm, colorist Jill Bogdanowicz |
| Year | 2012 |

### Why Super 16mm?

Anderson and Yeoman chose Super 16mm specifically for:
1. **Increased grain structure**: The visible grain gives a nostalgic, "home movie" texture.
2. **Softer inherent sharpness**: Super 16 has inherently less resolving power than 35mm, contributing to the non-clinical, storybook feel.
3. **Deeper depth of field**: The smaller format provides naturally deeper focus, keeping more of the highly-detailed sets in focus.
4. **Lighter cameras**: The smaller Aaton cameras allowed more fluid Steadicam work through the island terrain.

### Color Strategy

- **Warm yellow-green cast**: The entire film is bathed in an "end of summer" golden light.
- **Khaki Scout yellow**: The dominant costuming color, echoed in the wheat fields.
- **Punctuation reds**: Suzy's binoculars, the lighthouse, her dress accents — highly saturated against the muted warm background.
- **Pale blue sky**: Desaturated sky blue, slightly pushed toward cyan, creating the teal-warm split that defines the Anderson pastel look.
- **Color timing**: Jill Bogdanowicz at EFilm pushed the warmth heavily in the DI, emphasizing the Kodak film stock's natural warm bias.

### Lighting Approach
- Natural light emphasis: Many exteriors were shot with available light, supplemented with bounce and diffusion.
- Interiors used tungsten practicals (table lamps, candles) for warmth.
- The 1965 period setting uses period-accurate lighting (warm practical bulbs, not modern daylight-balanced LED).
- The famous "camp Khaki Scout" sequences used soft overhead diffusion to maintain the flat, storybook lighting.

### Camera Movement
- Extensive use of whip pans (rapid horizontal pans between characters).
- Slow-motion walking sequences.
- Static, symmetrical compositions for the most visually important moments.
- Dolly shots with characters centered in frame.
- The camera is almost always at 90 degrees or straight on — very few Dutch angles or dynamic angles.

## Asteroid City (2023)

### Cinematography Details

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Director of Photography | Robert Yeoman, ASC |
| Camera | Arricam LT, Arricam ST |
| Lenses | Cooke S4, Angenieux Optimo zooms |
| Film Stock | Kodak Vision3 35mm (likely 200T/250D) |
| Format | 35mm, multiple aspect ratios |
| DI / Color | Company 3 / EFilm |
| Year | 2023 |

### Visual Strategy

*Asteroid City* operates on two visual planes:

#### The TV Broadcast (B&W Academy 1.37:1)
- Framing device: a 1950s television documentary about the play's creation.
- Shot in black and white with hard, studio-style lighting.
- Bryan Cranston as the host, direct address to camera.

#### The Play (Widescreen Color 2.35:1)
- The "play within the TV show" is the color portion.
- Widescreen CinemaScope ratio, emulating 1950s Hollywood epics.
- Bright, pastel desert color palette.

### Color Strategy

The *Asteroid City* color palette is Anderson's most consciously "1950s" grade:

- **Atomic Teal**: The dominant color. The sky, architecture, and set pieces are heavily teal/turquoise. This references 1950s postcard photography and Kodachrome's teal bias.
- **Coral/Peach**: The warm counterpoint to the teal. Costuming (Scarlett Johansson's dresses), diner interiors, and signage use coral and peach tones.
- **Mint Green**: The soda fountain/diner details use 1950s diner mint green.
- **Desert Sand**: Warm beige/tan anchors the landscape.
- **1950s Pink**: Pastel pink in costuming and production design.

### Kodachrome-Influenced Grade
- Kodachrome film (Kodak's legendary 1950s slide film) had distinct characteristics: cool shadows, saturated midtones, and a slight cyan bias.
- The DI for *Asteroid City* emulates these characteristics digitally while shooting on Kodak negative stock.
- Skin tones are slightly cooler than Moonrise Kingdom — not as warm/golden.
- The grade references 1950s roadside Americana photography and Atomic Age illustration.

### Lighting Approach
- **Hard desert sun**: Unlike the soft lighting of Grand Budapest, Asteroid City uses harsh, direct sunlight to evoke the desert.
- **Soft fill on faces**: Actors are filled with bounce/reflectors to maintain soft skin despite the hard environment light.
- **Practical neon**: 1950s diner neon signage in teal and coral colors.
- **Mixed color temperatures**: Warm tungsten practicals inside the diner, cool daylight outside — the natural split-toning that defines the Anderson pastel aesthetic.

### Production Design
- The entire town was built as a practical set on a flat plain near Chinchón, Spain (standing in for the American Southwest).
- The iconic crater and observatory were physical builds, not CGI.
- All colors in frame are practical — painted sets, dyed costumes, colored props.
- The teal sky was enhanced but fundamentally exists in-camera through production design and filter choices.

### Camera Movement
- More static, tableau-like compositions than previous films.
- The "play" framing means the camera often stays in a proscenium-style fixed position.
- Tracking shots follow characters laterally across the wide frame.
- The ultrawide 2.35:1 ratio allows for highly structured compositions with multiple characters.

## Robert Yeoman, ASC — The Common Thread

All three films were shot by Robert Yeoman, who has photographed every live-action Wes Anderson film. His contributions to the pastel aesthetic:

### Yeoman's Approach (from interviews)
1. **Shoot the color in-camera**: Yeoman prioritizes getting the color palette right on set through production design, lighting gels, and costume choices. The DI enhances, it doesn't create.
2. **Film stock consistency**: Kodak Vision3 stocks are chosen for their highlight roll-off characteristics — the shoulder compression that gives Anderson's films their creamy highlights.
3. **Soft lighting philosophy**: Large sources, heavy diffusion, minimal contrast ratio. This is the lighting foundation the pastel grade sits on top of.
4. **Lens choice**: Cookes for warmth and roundness (Grand Budapest, Asteroid City), Zeiss Super Speeds for their period-appropriate look (Moonrise Kingdom).
5. **Handheld rarely used**: Almost everything is on sticks, dolly, or Steadicam — the formal precision is part of the visual language.

## Colorist: Jill Bogdanowicz

Jill Bogdanowicz at EFilm/Company 3 has been the colorist on multiple Anderson films. Her approach:
- Works closely with Yeoman during the DI to refine the in-camera palette.
- Pushes color temperature in specific directions per film (warm for Moonrise, cool for Asteroid City).
- Maintains the film stock's natural highlight roll-off — doesn't "break" it with aggressive digital grading.
- Uses power windows to selectively adjust specific color areas within a frame.

## Comparative Analysis: The Three Films

| Element | Grand Budapest (2014) | Moonrise Kingdom (2012) | Asteroid City (2023) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Dominant warm** | Pink, purple, gold | Yellow, khaki, warm wood | Coral, peach, sand |
| **Dominant cool** | Pastel blue, mint | Pale sky blue, green | Teal, turquoise, mint |
| **Saturation** | High (1930s), muted (1960s) | Medium, nostalgic | Medium-high, Kodachrome |
| **Contrast** | Very low (soft, filled) | Medium-low | Medium (desert sun harder) |
| **Film Stock** | 35mm (fine grain) | Super 16mm (visible grain) | 35mm (fine grain) |
| **Aspect Ratio** | Mixed: 1.37, 1.85, 2.35 | 1.85:1 | Mixed: 2.35, 1.37, 1.85 |
| **Color Era Ref** | 1930s Europe storybook | 1965 New England summer | 1955 Atomic Age Southwest |
| **Key Grade Move** | Heavy pink shift | Heavy yellow-green shift | Heavy teal-coral shift |
