# Fuji Superia 100 — Aesthetic Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## Overview

Fuji Superia 100 was the **fine-grain consumer color negative film** in Fujifilm's Superia lineup — the slower, sharper sibling to Superia 200, 400, 800, and 1600. It offered the characteristic Fuji consumer palette (cool bias, green shadows, magenta-reds) with significantly finer grain and higher resolving power thanks to its slower ISO. Discontinued around 2009, it's one of the earliest casualties of the digital transition and remains one of the less-documented Superia variants. This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates Superia 100.

## Core Aesthetic Signature

### The Classic Fuji Cool Cast

Like all Superia films, Superia 100 has a **cool-to-neutral daylight balance** that differentiates it from warm-biased Kodak consumer stocks. White surfaces render clean and slightly cool. The overall image feels crisp, modern, and slightly clinical — less "nostalgic" than Kodak and more "editorial."

### Fine Grain for a Consumer Film

At ISO 100, Superia 100 resolves more detail and shows less grain than any other Superia variant. Key grain characteristics:
- **Fine, tight grain** — barely visible at normal viewing distances
- Smoother than Superia 200 and dramatically finer than 400/800/1600
- More grain than professional ISO 100 films (Ektar, Reala) but excellent for consumer-grade
- Most visible in blue skies and smooth midtones

### The Fuji Green Shadow Cast

Superia 100 shares the family's signature greenish shadow rendering, though it's **less pronounced** than faster variants. The finer grain structure means the green cast appears as a subtle color shift rather than a textured murk.

### Magenta-Red Push

Like all Superia stocks, reds lean toward magenta/crimson rather than orange-red. Compared to Superia 400:
- Red saturation is similar but **cleaner** (less noisy)
- Magenta bias is preserved
- Skin tones benefit from the finer grain — less ruddy texture in cheeks

### Greens

- **Cool, authentic green** rendering characteristic of Fuji consumer films
- Foliage is rich but not pushed toward yellow (unlike Kodak warm-green)
- Finer grain means smoother green rendering
- Shadow greens are softer than Superia 400

### Blues

- Clean, crisp blue skies
- Deep blue saturation — characteristic of Fuji consumer dyes
- Less blue grain than faster Superia variants
- Slight cyan bias in cool scenes

### Skin Tones

- Neutral to slightly cool
- Finer grain produces smoother skin texture
- Less magenta cast than Superia 400 on faces
- Overall more flattering than faster variants for portraiture

### Contrast Profile

- **Moderate consumer contrast** — slightly higher than Superia 400
- Slower films typically have more inherent contrast
- Good midtone separation
- Clean shadow definition with less murk

### Exposure Latitude

- Good latitude within ±2 stops
- Overexposure by 1/3 to 2/3 stop recommended for best color
- Underexposure amplifies green shadow cast
- Box speed (ISO 100) produces clean, sharp results in good light

## Fuji X Weekly Recipe Data

The Fuji X Weekly community provides an in-camera recipe for Superia 100:
- Film Simulation: Classic Negative
- DR: Auto
- Grain: Weak, Small
- Color Chrome Effect: Strong
- Color Chrome FX Blue: Weak
- WB: Daylight, 0R -1B
- Highlight: -1, Shadow: -2
- Color: +1
- Sharpness: -2
- Clarity: -2
- Exposure: +1/3 to +2/3

## Best Use Cases

- Daylight landscape and nature photography
- Travel photography requiring fine detail
- Portraits needing cleaner rendering than faster Superia
- Flash photography (4th color layer benefits)
- Architecture and urban scenes

## Weaknesses

- Requires good light (ISO 100)
- Cool bias not ideal for warm/golden-hour scenes
- Discontinued and increasingly rare
- Less forgiving in flat/overcast light

## Summary

> Fuji Superia 100 produced images with the **classic Fuji consumer cool cast**, **very fine grain for its class**, **subtle greenish shadows**, and **magenta-biased reds**. It combined the Superia family's distinctive color palette with the resolving power of a slower emulsion — making it the cleanest, sharpest Superia variant and an excellent all-purpose daylight film.
