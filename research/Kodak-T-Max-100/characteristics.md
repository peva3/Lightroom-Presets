# Kodak T-Max 100 — Film Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## Overview
Kodak introduced T-Max 100 in 1986 alongside T-Max 400. It is a T-grain (tabular grain) black-and-white negative film known for exceptionally fine grain, high sharpness, and rich detail. T-Max films use flat, plate-like silver halide crystals that capture more light per surface area than traditional cubic-grain films. This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates T Max 100.

## Key Characteristics
- **ISO**: 100 (nominal)
- **Grain**: Extremely fine — among the finest-grain B&W films ever made
- **Sharpness**: Very high resolving power, excellent edge definition
- **Contrast**: Moderate to moderately high, depending on development
- **Tonal Range**: Clean, linear tonality with good highlight/shadow separation
- **Development**: Responds well to T-Max developer; slightly more finicky than traditional films — precise time/temp control matters
- **Exposure Latitude**: Good but narrower than Tri-X; benefits from precise exposure

## Historical Context
- Introduced 1986, updated in 2007 for finer grain and improved sharpness
- Divided the photographic community: T-Max fans vs. Tri-X fans
- T-Max = clean, precise, modern; Tri-X = gritty, forgiving, classic
- Still in production as of 2024 (Kodak Alaris)

## Visual Signature
- Clean, "technical" look
- Very fine grain structure almost invisible at normal enlargement sizes
- Excellent for landscapes, architecture, studio portraiture
- Less "character" than traditional-grain films — prized for neutrality and detail

## Comparison to T-Max 400
- T-Max 100 has noticeably finer grain than T-Max 400
- Slightly different contrast curve — T-Max 100 has marginally more contrast
- Shares the same T-grain technology and general tonal character
- Both are sharper and finer-grained than Tri-X at equivalent speeds
