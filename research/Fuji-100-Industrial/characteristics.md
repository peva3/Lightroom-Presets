## Historical Context

Fujicolor 100 Industrial was a Japan domestic-market-only film sold primarily in bulk rolls to convenience stores, photo booths, and volume portrait studios. It was re-branded Fujicolor 100 (Japan version), which differed from the Fujicolor 100 sold internationally.

The film's most unusual characteristic is its emulsion design: a **tungsten-balanced emulsion (3200K)** that achieves daylight balance through dye color shifts in the emulsion layers. This creates a unique color signature — slightly cool overall with subtle red-channel warmth that doesn't read as a conventional white balance shift.

Discontinued as Fujifilm consolidated its consumer film lines. The industrial bulk packaging made it a favorite among Japanese budget-conscious photographers who could purchase it by the 100-roll case at significantly reduced prices.

## Unique Emulsion Design

The tungsten-balanced emulsion with daylight dyes creates a signature that's hard to replicate with simple WB adjustments:

- **Base sensitivity**: 3200K (tungsten)
- **Color balance achieved via**: Dye couplers shifted to compensate
- **Net effect**: Cool-neutral rendering with subtle warm undertones in skin
- **Red channel**: Slightly elevated due to tungsten base sensitivity
- **Blue channel**: Controlled through dye shift (not a simple WB adjustment)

## Key Color Signatures

1. **Cool-neutral balance**: Not cold, not warm — a distinctive in-between.
2. **Mild saturation**: Colors are present but never loud. Gentle, restrained.
3. **Skin tones**: Cool-neutral but not pale. Subtle warmth from tungsten base.
4. **Greens**: Mildly desaturated, clean, slightly toward yellow.
5. **Blues**: Clean and controlled — not the aggressive cyan of some Fuji films.
6. **Grain**: Essentially invisible at any normal viewing size.
7. **Highlights**: Bright but controlled — Highlight +1 preserves detail while adding brightness.

## Fuji X Weekly Recipe (2019)

The [original Fuji X Weekly recipe](https://fujixweekly.com/2019/06/13/) was one of Ritchie Roesch's earliest film simulation recipes, using PRO Neg. Std as the base. The WB 3200K +8 Red -8 Blue directly recreates the film's unique tungsten-balanced emulsion with daylight dyes. Roesch notes the recipe is "especially good in higher-contrast scenes."
