# Michael Kenna

## Biography
- **Name**: Michael Kenna (born 1953)
- **Nationality**: British
- **Era**: 1970s–present

## Philosophy
- Minimalist landscape photography — less is everything
- Long exposure as a way of seeing what the eye cannot
- Dawn and night shooting exclusively — no daytime work
- "You can't always see what's there — you have to wait for it to reveal itself"
- Inspired by Japanese aesthetics (wabi-sabi, haiku) and Chinese landscape painting

## Technical Approach
- **Gear**: Hasselblad medium format, Holga plastic cameras
- **Film**: Kodak Tri-X 320 (sheet film), Ilford Delta 100
- **Exposures**: 30 seconds to 10+ hours — the defining technique
- **Processing**: Standard B&W chemistry; printing is where the magic happens
- **Printing**: Selenium-toned gelatin silver prints; split-toning in darkroom

## Visual Signature
- **Long exposure smoothness**: Water becomes mist, clouds become streaks, people vanish
- **Extreme minimalism**: Often a single tree, a pier, a mountain against empty sky
- **High contrast with smooth transitions**: Deep blacks against bright skies, but gradients are silky
- **Dreamy atmosphere**: The long exposure creates an otherworldly stillness
- **No human presence**: Even urban scenes look abandoned and timeless
- **Square format**: Consistent use of Hasselblad 6x6

## Iconic Images
- *Ratcliffe Power Station* series
- *Hokkaido* series
- *Mont St. Michel* series

## Preset Design Notes
- Very high contrast to emulate the stark minimalism
- Smooth highlight rolloff — no clipping despite bright values
- No grain — long exposures on medium format are virtually grainless
- Shadows lifted enough to show form, but overall dramatic
- This preset works best on images with strong negative space and geometry
