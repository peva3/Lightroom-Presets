# Ilford HP5 Plus Lightroom Preset — Full Aesthetic Characteristics

> A comprehensive breakdown of HP5's visual signature, with extended HP5 vs Tri-X analysis.

---

## The "British Tri-X" — Context

The Ilford HP5 Plus Lightroom preset recreates this look digitally using Lightroom's color grading tools for authentic film emulation. Ilford HP5 Plus is often called the "British Tri-X" — the workhorse of B&W film outside North America. The nickname captures its role (go-to ISO 400 film for professionals and amateurs alike) but undersells its distinct aesthetic personality.

### Lineage
- **HP** = "Hypersensitive Panchromatic" — dates to 1931 Ilford glass plates
- **HP3** (1941–1969): The classic; pushed to ISO 400 in 1960
- **HP4** (1965–1976): Improved sharpness and finer grain
- **HP5** (1976–1989): ISO 400, new emulsion technology
- **HP5 Plus** (1989–present): Enhanced emulsion, better push performance, current formulation

HP5+ has been continuously improved but the fundamental aesthetic character — *softer contrast + open midtones + classic cubic grain* — has been consistent since the HP5 era.

## Core Aesthetic Signature

### 1. Contrast: Softer than Tri-X

HP5's defining characteristic is **lower intrinsic contrast** than Kodak Tri-X 400.

| Property | HP5 Plus | Tri-X 400 |
|---|---|---|
| Film gamma (ID-11/D-76 stock) | ~0.55–0.58 | ~0.62–0.65 |
| Shoulder onset | Later (more highlight headroom) | Earlier (more highlight compression) |
| Toe | Longer, softer — holds shadow detail further down | Shorter, steeper — shadows go black sooner |
| Overall contrast impression | "Medium" per Ilford | "Medium-high" in practice |
| Print grade equivalent (condenser) | Grade 2.5–3 | Grade 2–2.5 |

**Practically**: HP5 gives you more to work with in post/printing. The negative is "thinner" — less dense in highlights, more open in shadows. This means:
- Easier scanning (less D-max for scanners to penetrate)
- More editing flexibility (less baked-in contrast)
- Better shadow recovery in digital post
- Requires more contrast added in post/printing — HP5 is a "contrast-flexible" film

### 2. Mid-Tone Richness — The "HP5 Grey Range"

HP5's most praised characteristic is its **extended grey range**, particularly in the mid-tones (Zones IV–VI). This is where HP5 outperforms Tri-X.

- Where Tri-X transitions midtones with a certain crispness/snap, HP5 draws them out smoothly
- Skin tones on HP5 show more **subtle gradation** — the cheek-to-shadow transition on a face has more visible steps
- HP5 produces "creamier" midtones — less harsh, more fluid
- The grey range is why portrait photographers often prefer HP5 over Tri-X for available-light work

**Visual metaphor**: HP5 midtones are like charcoal drawing — continuous gradation. Tri-X midtones are like ink wash — distinct, graphic separation.

### 3. Grain: Gentler, Tighter, "Classic Cubic"

HP5 uses traditional **cubic-grain** technology (unlike Ilford's own Delta films which use epitaxial/tabular grains). This gives classic, "salt-and-pepper" grain.

| Grain Property | HP5 Plus | Tri-X 400 |
|---|---|---|
| RMS granularity | ~10 (ID-11 stock) | ~11 (D-76 stock) |
| Grain appearance | Fine, tight, "clean" for a 400 film | Slightly larger, more "textured" |
| Grain in highlights | Smooth, only visible in large prints | More visible, characteristically gritty |
| Grain in midtones | Transitions smoothly — less obvious | More palpable — part of the texture |
| Grain at 35mm (8×10) | Visible but not dominant | Clearly visible, part of the look |
| Grain at 120 (11×14) | Very fine, often invisible | Fine, sometimes visible in skies |

**Key difference**: HP5 grain is softer-edged than Tri-X grain. In digital scans, HP5 grain looks "gentler" — Tri-X grain has more edge definition and "crunch." This makes HP5 more flattering for portraits and smooth-tone subjects; Tri-X adds texture that benefits street/reportage work.

### 4. Spectral Sensitivity

HP5 is panchromatic with slightly extended red sensitivity compared to Tri-X:

| Wavelength | HP5 Response | Tri-X Response | Visual Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blue (450nm) | Moderate | Higher | HP5 skies less sensitive → slightly lighter blue skies without filter |
| Green (550nm) | Peak sensitivity | Peak sensitivity | Both render foliage naturally |
| Yellow (580nm) | Strong | Strong | Both lighten yellow/orange naturally |
| Red (630nm) | Extended | Moderate | HP5 renders caucasian skin slightly lighter/brighter |
| Deep red (660nm+) | Sharp cutoff | Sharp cutoff | Neither has usable IR sensitivity |

**Practical result**:
- HP5 skin tones (caucasian): slightly lighter, more luminous — flattering for portraits
- HP5 skies (no filter): slightly lighter grey than Tri-X — less dramatic
- HP5 red objects: slightly lighter than Tri-X
- Both respond similarly to yellow/orange filters for sky darkening

### 5. Push Behavior

HP5 was formulated to push well — the datasheet explicitly recommends push processing up to EI 3200.

| EI | HP5 Look | Tri-X Look |
|---|---|---|
| 400 (box) | Balanced, open, forgiving | Snappy, contrasty, punchy |
| 800 (+1) | Slightly more contrast, controlled grain | Noticeably more contrast, grain picking up |
| 1600 (+2) | Gritty, dramatic, still some shadow detail | Heavy contrast, shadows go black, "classic pushed" |
| 3200 (+3) | Heavy grain, high contrast, usable | Very heavy grain, extreme contrast, "effect" territory |

**HP5 pushes more gracefully** — it holds shadow detail better at high EIs, and the grain increase is more gradual. Tri-X at +2 and +3 becomes a strong aesthetic statement; HP5 remains more flexible.

| Developer | Contrast | Grain | Sharpness | "HP5 Signatures" |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| **ID-11 1+1** | Medium-low | Fine | Better | More open shadows, slightly sharper, one-shot convenience |
| **ID-11 1+3** | Low | Slightly larger | Maximum | Stand/high-dilution look — edge effects, apparent sharpness |

## HP5 vs Tri-X: The Classic Debate

### The Short Answer
- **HP5**: Softer, smoother, more forgiving, bigger grey range. Elegant.
- **Tri-X**: Crisper, grainier, more contrast, more "bite." Bold.

### Perceived Strengths by Genre

| Genre | HP5 Advantage | Tri-X Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| **Portrait** | Creamy mid-tones, smooth skin, softer grain | Harder contrast can add drama |
| **Street** | Forgiving of mixed/bad light | Crisp shadows = graphic punch |
| **Documentary** | Wide latitude, scan-friendly | Classic reportage look |
| **Landscape** | Extended grey range, holds cloud detail | Dramatic skies, strong separation |
| **Available light** | Better shadow retention at EI 800-1600 | More contrast compensates for flat light |
| **Studio/controlled light** | More tonality to work with | May be too contrasty without pull |
| **Scanning** | Flatter negative = more digital latitude | Denser negative = some scanners struggle |
| **Darkroom printing** | Often needs grade 3 or split-grade | Prints beautifully at grade 2 |
| **Pushing** | Holds shadow detail better, more usable | More dramatic look when pushed |
| **Pulling** | HP5 at EI 200 is beautiful — Tri-X at 200 is also excellent | Both pull well |

### Key Characteristics Comparison Table

| Attribute | Ilford HP5 Plus | Kodak Tri-X 400 |
|---|---|---|
| **Film base** | Grey-tinted acetate (35mm) | Grey-tinted acetate (35mm) |
| **Speed** | ISO 400 (true speed ~400) | ISO 400 (true speed ~320-400) |
| **Contrast (IE)** | 0.55 (lower) | 0.62 (higher) |
| **D-max** | ~1.8 (ID-11 stock) | ~2.0 (D-76 stock) |
| **Shoulder** | Longer, gentler roll-off | Steeper, compresses sooner |
| **Toe** | Longer, softer | Shorter, steeper |
| **Shadow detail (Zone I-II)** | More separation | Less separation, darker |
| **Midtone (Zone IV-VI)** | Wide, smooth transition | Crisp, snappy transition |
| **Highlight (Zone VII-IX)** | More headroom, retains detail | Compresses, burns out sooner |
| **Grain (RMS)** | ~10 | ~11 |
| **Grain character** | Soft-edged, "gentle" | Sharp-edged, "crisp" |
| **Sharpness (MTF)** | Slightly lower at high frequencies | Slightly higher at high frequencies |
| **Red sensitivity** | Slightly extended | Standard panchromatic |
| **Blue sensitivity** | Moderate | Higher |
| **Push to 1600** | More controlled | More dramatic |
| **Push to 3200** | Usable | Extreme |
| **Curling** | Minimal (polyester 120 base) | Moderate (acetate base) |
| **Availability** | 35mm, 120, sheets (4×5, 8×10) | 35mm, 120, sheets (4×5, 8×10) |
| **Price (35mm, 2024)** | ~$8-9/roll | ~$10-12/roll |
| **Price (120, 2024)** | ~$7-8/roll | ~$9-11/roll |

## Aesthetic Summary

### What HP5 Does Well
1. **Available-light portraits** — smooth skin tones, soft roll-off, forgiving shadows
2. **Overcast/diffuse light** — contrast is naturally low; HP5 retains mid-tone separation without going flat
3. **Mixed lighting** — wide latitude handles varied illumination in the same frame
4. **Scanning** — thinner negative scans better; more editable digital file
5. **Pushing** — retains shadow detail better at EI 800 and 1600
6. **Forgiveness** — hard to mess up exposure with HP5; ±2 stops is recoverable
7. **Beginners** — Ilford's recommended starter film; teaches exposure without punishing

### What HP5 Doesn't Do Well
1. **Punchy "street" contrast at box speed** — needs development or post adjustment
2. **Dramatic skies without filters** — blue sensitivity is moderate; skies render lighter
3. **"Classic Tri-X grain aesthetic"** — if you want big, crunchy grain, Rodinal + Tri-X is the recipe
4. **Very fine grain at 35mm** — Delta 400 or T-Max 400 are cleaner choices if grain is unwanted
5. **Maximum sharpness** — T-grain films (Delta, T-Max) resolve more detail

### The HP5 "Look" in Five Words
> **Smooth, versatile, forgiving, classic, elegant.**

### The Tri-X "Look" in Five Words  
> **Bold, gritty, punchy, iconic, dramatic.**

## Scanning HP5 — Best Practices

### Flatbed (Epson V-series)
- Scan as B&W negative (16-bit greyscale)
- Epson Scan: Set histogram to capture full range without clipping
- SilverFast: NegaFix profile for HP5 works well
- Expect flatter scan than Tri-X — add contrast in post, not in scan

### DSLR/Mirrorless Scanning
- Use a flat-field macro lens (1:1)
- Expose to retain all highlight detail — HP5 highlights are recoverable
- Convert using green channel for cleanest mono, or combine channels using NLP
- White balance off clear film base (not orange mask — HP5 has grey base)

### Lab Scans (Noritsu/Frontier)
- Noritsu scans: Usually flatter, more neutral
- Frontier scans: Slightly more contrast baked in
- Request "flat" scans if you intend to edit extensively
- 16-bit TIFF is worth the extra cost for HP5 — mid-tones have more data

### Tone Curve for HP5 Scans
```
Ideal HP5 scan curve (digital post):
1. Lift shadows slightly (+10-15) — open the toe
2. Anchor midtones — preserve the HP5 grey range
3. Gentle highlight roll-off (−5 to −10) — prevent blown highlights
4. Deepen blacks (−10 to −15) — add targeted contrast
```
This creates the "open negative" look HP5 is known for without losing the contrast needed for a finished image.

## HP5 in Different Formats

| Format | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **35mm (135)** | Classic look. Grain visible from ~8×10. Smooth for a 400-speed 35mm film. Most popular format. |
| **120 (Medium Format)** | Much finer grain — nearly grainless at 11×14. Tonality is extraordinary — HP5's grey range really shines. Many photographers call 120 HP5 "the perfect B&W film." |
| **4×5 Sheet** | Virtually grainless below 16×20. Tonality is the star — the combination of HP5's curve + large format's resolution is special. Reciprocity characteristics become relevant. |
| **8×10 Sheet** | Contact-print territory. Grain is irrelevant. Tonal gradation at its maximum. |

## Final Verdict: HP5's Place in B&W Photography

HP5 Plus occupies a unique position: it is simultaneously the **beginner's recommended film** (forgiving, available, affordable) AND a **professional's choice** (flexible, predictable, beautiful tonality). Few films span this range.

Its softer contrast and more open shadows make it the **"editor's film"** — it gives you more to work with. Tri-X gives you more baked-in character. Neither is better; they are different tools.

The "British Tri-X" nickname is both apt and misleading. HP5 is the British workhorse equivalent, but it produces images with a distinctly different emotional character: **calmer, smoother, more elegant** where Tri-X is bolder, grittier, and more dramatic.
