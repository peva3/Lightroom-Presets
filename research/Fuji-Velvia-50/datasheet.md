## Grain & Sharpness

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **RMS Granularity** | 9 |
| **Resolving Power** | Up to 160 lines/mm (35mm) — **highest of any slide film** |
| **MTF Peak** | 120% at 10 cycles/mm — gives Velvia its characteristic "bite" and edge contrast |

The MTF exceeding 100% at 10 lp/mm means Velvia exhibits **edge enhancement** at mid spatial frequencies. This is key to its perceived sharpness and "pop" on a light table.

---

## Spectral Sensitivity & Color Balance

Velvia 50 is balanced for **daylight (5500K)** . It uses multiple dye layers optimized for:

- **Deep, saturated greens** — enhanced sensitivity in the green spectral region with suppressed cyan cross-talk
- **Punchy blues** — high blue-channel saturation, sky rendering is deep and dramatic, especially with underexposure
- **Warm-biased yellows → reds** — yellows are shifted toward red/warmer tones, giving golden-hour light a spectacular warmth
- **Magenta-tending reds** — pure reds have a slight magenta bias, making flowers and warm tones vivid
- **Skin tones** — NOT neutral; faces appear ruddy/sunburned. Use Velvia 100 or Astia for portraits
- **Shadow color** — Deep neutrals trending slightly cool (Velvia's blacks are legendary — highest dMax of any slide film)

### Color Correction Layers (RVP50)

The 2007 reformulation introduced color correction interlayers originally developed for Provia 100F:
- DIR couplers regulate interlayer effects
- Edge enhancement between dye layers improves color separation
- Better reciprocity characteristics than original RVP

---

## Characteristic Curves

### dMax (Maximum Density)

Velvia 50 achieves a **dMax of ~3.8–4.0**, the deepest blacks of any color slide film. This gives:
- Highest dynamic range when projected
- Deep, "inky" blacks on a light table
- Extraordinary contrast and "pop"

### Exposure Latitude

**±½ stop** — extremely narrow. Velvia is unforgiving:
- Overexposure blows highlights to clear film base (no detail recovery)
- Underexposure produces inky, blocked shadows but deep, saturated colors
- Many photographers rate at **EI 40 or even 32** to open shadows slightly

### Contrast

Very high. Velvia compresses the tonal scale, emphasizing mid-tone contrast:
- Highlights clip abruptly
- Shadows crush to deep black
- Mid-tones are punchy and separated

## Reciprocity Failure

### Original RVP (1990–2005)
Extremely poor — unusable beyond ~8 seconds without magenta CC filtration:

| Meter Reading | Actual Exposure |
|---|---|
| 1s | 1s |
| 2s | 2.5s |
| 4s | 5s |
| 8s | 12s |
| 15s | 25s |
| 30s | 1m |
| 1m | 2.5m |
| 2m | 6m |
| 4m | 13m |
| 8m | 30m |

Beyond 4s requires **+5M to +10M** color correction filter for neutral balance. Exposures over 64 seconds are "not recommended" by Fuji.

### RVP50 (2007–present)
Improved reciprocity but still significant. Better than original RVP, worse than Velvia 100 (which needs no compensation under 1 minute).

---

## Dye Stability & Archival

- Dark storage: ~60+ years before noticeable fading (per Fuji accelerated testing)
- Projection: Significantly better than Kodachrome for projection stability
- Modern Fujichrome films hold color accuracy for years of archival storage
- **Critical**: Keep frozen (-10°F / -23°C) for long-term storage; room-temperature storage causes magenta shift within 1–2 years past expiry

## Key Optical Properties Summary

| Characteristic | Behavior |
|---|---|
| Green rendering | Hyper-saturated, deep emerald/forest tones. Blues in foliage suppressed. |
| Blue rendering | Intense, cyan-suppressed. Skies go deep blue, almost polarizer-like naturally. |
| Red rendering | Vivid, slightly magenta. Warm colors amplified. |
| Yellow rendering | Warmer than neutral (red-shifted). Golden hour magic. |
| Skin tones | Poor — ruddy, over-saturated. Use 81A filter in shade. |
| Dynamic range | 5–6 stops on film; narrowest of any color slide film |
| Contrast | Very high. Crushes shadows, clips highlights. |
| Sharpness | Highest resolving power of any slide film. Edge-enhanced MTF. |

---

## Fujifilm's Digital "Velvia" Film Simulation

Fuji's in-camera "Velvia/Vivid" film simulation has been widely criticized (including by Ken Rockwell) as a poor representation of actual Velvia film. It was reportedly designed to match **scans** of Velvia, not the film itself viewed on a light table. It lacks:
- The deep dMax / inky blacks
- The warm yellow→red bias
- The unique MTF edge enhancement
