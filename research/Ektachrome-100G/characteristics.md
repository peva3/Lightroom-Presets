# Ektachrome 100G — Aesthetic Breakdown

> Predecessor to current E100, slightly warmer blue, finer grain. Professional standard for commercial work 2001-2012.

---

## Core Identity

Kodak Ektachrome 100G was introduced in 2001 as the professional-grade successor to Ektachrome 100S and Ektachrome 100SW. It was the workhorse transparency film of the digital transition era — used by commercial, editorial, and advertising photographers who needed a reliable, accurate slide film with excellent scan-to-print workflow.

When Kodak discontinued Ektachrome in 2012, 100G was the last professional Ektachrome standing. The E100 revival in 2018 was a different formulation entirely — warmer, slightly less blue, and with coarser grain than 100G. Photographers who remember 100G note it was a "more precise" film than current E100.

---

## Color Palette

### Blues — The Defining Characteristic
- E100G rendered blues with a unique "deep and clean" character
- A slight warmth in the blue channel — not the cyan cool of Kodachrome, but a rich, slightly warm blue
- Skies were deep but not dramatic — meant for accurate reproduction, not landscape drama
- Perfect for product photography with blue backgrounds or blue electronics

### Neutrals — Reference Quality
- E100G was the most neutral slide film Kodak ever made
- Greys rendered with exceptional accuracy — this was essential for catalog and product work
- White balance was dead-on daylight (5500K) with no perceptible warm or cool cast
- This neutrality made it the preferred film for high-end commercial reproduction

### Reds — Accurate and Clean
- Reds rendered accurately without the orange bias of Kodachrome or the magenta bias of Velvia
- Essential for fashion and product photography where fabric/logo color accuracy mattered
- Slightly desaturated relative to E100VS (the "Vivid Saturation" variant)

### Greens — Slightly Blue-Biased
- Cool, natural greens without the yellow-green of warmer films
- Product photography with foliage or outdoor elements looked clean and modern
- Not as lush as Fuji's slide films but more accurate

### Skin Tones — Cool But Accurate
- Skin was rendered slightly cooler than most slide films
- Commercial photographers could warm in-camera (filtration) or in post (scanning)
- The cool neutrality was actually preferred for catalog work where color accuracy trumped "flattering"

## Grain & Sharpness

- **RMS 8**: Finer grain than current E100 (RMS 8 vs. E100's ~11). For a 100-speed film, this was exceptional.
- **T-Grain technology**: Kodak's tabular grain emulsion produced exceptionally uniform grain structure
- **Sharpness**: Very high resolving power with excellent MTF at moderate spatial frequencies
- **Scanning**: E100G was designed to scan well — the grain structure was optimized for drum scanners, which were the commercial standard in 2001-2012

---

## Contrast & Dynamic Range

- **Moderate contrast**: Lower than Velvia, similar to Provia
- **Clean highlight rolloff**: Engineered for product photography with specular highlights
- **About 6-7 stops**: Standard slide film dynamic range
- **Shadow detail**: Better than consumer-grade Ektachrome, not as good as color negative

## E100G vs. Current E100 (2018 revival)

| Characteristic | Ektachrome 100G | Ektachrome E100 (current) |
|---|---|---|
| Era | 2001-2012 | 2018-present |
| Color balance | Neutral-cool, slightly blue | Neutral-warm, slightly gold |
| Blue rendering | Deep clean blue, warm-biased | Clean blue, slightly cooler |
| Grain | RMS 8 (finer) | RMS ~11 (coarser) |
| Skin tones | Cool, accurate | Warm, flattering |
| Saturation | Moderate, accurate | Moderate, slightly higher |
| Contrast | Moderate | Moderate |
| Market | Professional commercial | Enthusiast/hobbyist |

## Why It Was the Professional Standard

E100G dominated commercial photography because:
1. **Color accuracy**: Product colors had to match — E100G didn't impose a "look"
2. **Scan-friendly**: Designed for the drum scanner → CMYK print workflow
3. **Consistent**: Batch-to-batch consistency was excellent (Kodak's pro film QC was top-tier)
4. **Neutral foundation**: Art directors could add their desired color grade in post, starting from an accurate base

## Digital Emulation Notes

- The t3mujinpack reference: neutral baseline with slightly warm highlight and blue shadow
- HSL: Subtle blue saturation boost, minimal other adjustments (neutral film)
- Grain: Very fine — similar to Velvia 100's grain level
- Color grading: Cool shadows (H215) with neutral-warm highlights — the "slightly warm blue"
- This is the most "minimal" slide preset — the E100G look is about accuracy, not character
- Key differentiator from current E100: Cooler, finer grain, more clinical color
