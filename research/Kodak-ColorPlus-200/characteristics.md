# Kodak ColorPlus 200 — Aesthetic Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## Overview

Kodak ColorPlus 200 is Kodak's **entry-level consumer color negative film** — a budget-oriented ISO 200 stock that delivers warm, golden-toned images with moderate contrast and visible grain. Often compared to Gold 200 (its premium consumer sibling), ColorPlus is the more affordable option with slightly muddier shadows, warmer overall tone, and a nostalgic 1990s drugstore aesthetic. It remains in production today as Kodak's cheapest color film. This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates ColorPlus 200.

## Core Aesthetic Signature

### The Golden-Warm Consumer Cast

ColorPlus 200 runs warm — noticeably warmer than neutral, with a distinct golden-yellow bias in midtones and highlights. White surfaces render with a cream/ivory cast. This is the "sunny nostalgia" look that defined pre-digital family photo albums.

Compared to Gold 200:
- **Slightly warmer** midtone temperature
- **More muted** overall saturation
- **Muddier, browner** shadows (less teal/cyan than Gold)
- **Similar grain** structure but slightly softer
- **Lower contrast** by a small margin

### Shadow Character

Where Gold 200 shows teal-cyan in the shadows, ColorPlus 200 shadows lean **warm brown**. This is the key differentiator — Gold has a complementary cool shadow, while ColorPlus stays warm throughout the tonal range. This makes ColorPlus feel more "cozy" and less "cinematic" than Gold.

### Grain Structure

Consumer-grade grain:
- **RMS granularity**: ~9 (estimated from Gold 200's 8-9 range)
- Visible at normal viewing distances in 35mm
- Slightly coarser and more irregular than Gold 200
- Pleasant organic texture rather than harsh or digital-feeling
- Most visible in midtones and skies

### Red Rendering

- **Warm, orange-leaning reds** rather than true crimson
- Less saturated than Gold 200 or Agfa Vista
- Skin tones benefit from the warm-red rendering — natural and flattering
- Reds in shadow areas take on a brownish cast

### Green Rendering

- Moderately desaturated greens
- Yellow-biased (not cool or blue-green)
- Foliage has a warm, golden cast
- Less "Fuji green" than any Fuji consumer stock

### Blue Rendering

- Muted blues — skies are pleasant but not hyper-saturated
- Slightly warm-blue bias (not the cool-cyan of Fuji consumer films)
- Blue shadows are brown-tinted

### Skin Tones

- **Warm and flattering** — the golden bias favors caucasian skin
- Slightly yellow in lighter skin tones
- Not accurate for all skin types — the warm bias is strong
- Community notes: "Very pleasing skin for a cheap film"

### Contrast Profile

- **Moderate consumer contrast** — snappy enough for drugstore prints
- Less contrast than Gold 200
- Shadows compress gently, not hard-clipped
- Highlights hold detail well for a budget stock
- Best exposed at box speed or +1/3 stop

### Exposure Latitude

- **Good for a budget film** — handles +1 to +2 stops overexposure
- Underexposure produces muddy shadows and color shifts
- Pushing not recommended beyond +1 stop
- EI 100 (overexposed 1 stop) warms the image further and softens grain

## The "Drugstore Film" Character

ColorPlus 200 was designed for:
- Point-and-shoot cameras with basic metering
- Minilab automated printing with no color correction
- Bright, sunny conditions (where the warm cast looks intentional)
- Flash photography (warm flash-look)

Its consumer DNA means it has built-in "pop" for straight-from-lab prints, but less sophistication than professional films. This unpretentious quality is precisely what makes it beloved today.

## Best Use Cases

- Golden hour and sunset photography
- Portraits in warm light
- Beach and summer scenes
- Nostalgic/vintage aesthetic
- Budget film experimentation

## Weaknesses

- Muddier shadows than Gold 200
- Overly warm for overcast or cool scenes
- Less accurate color rendition than professional films
- Visible grain limits enlargement potential
- Blue skies render duller than other consumer stocks

## Summary

> Kodak ColorPlus 200 produces images with a **warm golden-yellow cast**, **moderate consumer contrast**, **visible organic grain**, and **muted but pleasant color saturation**. Shadows lean warm brown rather than the teal-cyan of Gold 200. It renders skin tones warmly and flatteringly. The overall look is **sunny, nostalgic, and unpretentious** — the quintessential drugstore consumer film from the golden age of analog point-and-shoot photography.
