# Kodak Ektachrome E100 — Aesthetic Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## The Ektachrome Family

### E100 (2018–present)
The current Ektachrome E100 was reformulated from previous generations using modern emulsion technology while maintaining the classic Ektachrome look. Kodak describes it as "clean, crisp, with a neutral color balance." This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates Ektachrome E100.

### E100G (Grain — discontinued ca. 2012)
The predecessor to the current E100. "G" stood for optimized grain structure. E100G had a slightly warmer default white balance than the new E100, with marginally lower contrast. Very fine grain (RMS 8). Generally considered the most neutral of the Ektachrome family.

### E100VS (Vivid Saturation — discontinued ca. 2012)
The punchy sibling. E100VS had significantly boosted saturation across all channels, particularly in reds, yellows, and greens. Warmer default white balance. Higher contrast. Popular for landscape and nature photography. Less forgiving with skin tones — could render caucasian skin excessively red/magenta.

### E100S / E100SW (discontinued earlier)
- **E100S:** "Saturation" — mid-point between E100G and E100VS
- **E100SW:** "Saturation Warm" — warmer-balanced variant for portraiture/wedding

## Core Aesthetic of Current E100 (2018)

### 1. Cool-Leaning Blues
E100's single most distinctive characteristic. Where Fuji slide films (Provia, Velvia) tend toward a subtle warmth or magenta bias in the shadows, E100 leans **cool** — particularly evident in:

- **Skies:** Deep, slightly cyan-leaning blues. Not teal, not indigo — a cool, clean cerulean.
- **Shadows:** Neutral-to-cool. Not blue-cast, but definitely cooler than E100G or Provia.
- **White balance:** Daylight-balanced 5500K, but renders subjectively cooler than Provia 100F at equivalent lighting.
- **Open shade:** Pronounced blue shift — often requiring an 81A warming filter for portraits.

This coolness gives E100 a "modern," "architectural," "editorial" look compared to the warmer, nostalgia-evoking Kodak color negative films (Portra, Gold).

### 2. Clean, Modern Slide Look
The 2018 reformulation prioritized a **clean, clinical rendering**:

- **Neutrals are truly neutral.** Grays, whites, and blacks show no significant color cast at correct exposure.
- **No "vintage" warmth or tone curve compression.** This is not Portra. E100 renders linearly through the midtones.
- **Fine grain (RMS 8).** Nearly invisible at 35mm, imperceptible at 120/4x5. Comparable sharpness to Ektar 100 (negative film).
- **High acutance (edge sharpness).** The thin emulsion layers produce exceptionally sharp edges — scans look "digital" in their crispness.
- **White rendition:** Whites are pure, brilliant — "pop" off the light table. No cream or warmth.

### 3. Strong Contrast, Deep Blacks
Reversal (slide) film inherently has higher contrast than negative film:

- **D-max of ~3.2–3.3:** Blacks are genuinely black, not dark gray. Projected or scanned, shadows fall to true black.
- **S-curve character:** The midtones are linear (gamma ~1.8–1.9), but the toe and shoulder compress more steeply than color negative film.
- **Shadow detail:** Limited to approximately 2–3 stops below mid-gray. Anything below that goes to black. This is the "slide film look" — shadows are shadows, not zones of recoverable detail.
- **Highlight handling:** Bright highlights hold separation well up to ~+1.5 stops above mid-gray, then clip gracefully (no ugly digital-style blowout). Sunlit white surfaces retain texture better than most negative films when properly exposed.
- **Overall dynamic range:** ~7–8 stops usable (compared to ~12–14 for modern negative film, ~14+ for modern digital sensors).

### 4. Color Palette by Hue

| Hue | E100 Rendering | Comparison |
|-----|---------------|------------|
| **Blue (sky)** | Deep, slightly cyan. Cool but not teal. | Cooler than Provia; less saturated than Velvia |
| **Blue (shadow)** | Neutral-cool. Subtle cyan undertone. | Cleaner than Portra 400 (which goes warm/amber) |
| **Red** | True red, moderate saturation. Not orange-biased. | Less warm than E100VS or Gold 200; cleaner than Portra |
| **Orange/Yellow** | Accurate, moderate. Foliage yellows clean. | Less golden than Gold 200; less warm than Ektar |
| **Green (foliage)** | Slightly cool green — subtle teal edge. Not yellow-green. | Cleaner/cooler than Portra; less vivid than Velvia |
| **Green (artificial)** | Accurate, neutral. | Good for mixed lighting |
| **Magenta/Purple** | Clean, not overbearing. | Less magenta than Provia 100F |
| **Skin tones (light)** | Neutral, slight coolness. Can look pale. | Needs warming filter in shade; cooler than Portra |
| **Skin tones (dark)** | Rich, accurate. Handles darker skin well. | More flattering than E100VS |

### 5. The "Glassy" Quality
As described by film lab technician Duncan Gammon (SilverPan Film Lab) and echoed by photograhers — E100 has a **"glassy" or "vitreous"** rendering quality. This comes from:
- The smooth, grainless midtones
- The steep contrast transition at the shadow boundary
- The clean specular highlight rendering
- The slight Zeiss-like coolness

This quality makes E100 popular for:
- Architecture and urban landscapes
- Product/still life photography
- Fashion editorial (clean, modern)
- Landscapes where blue skies are desirable
- Any work where "clean and modern" trumps "warm and nostalgic"

### 6. Color Temperature Sensitivity
Slide film's fixed color balance means lighting temperature has a dramatic effect:

| Lighting | E100 Rendering | Mitigation |
|----------|---------------|------------|
| Daylight (5500K) | Neutral, clean | None needed |
| Late afternoon / golden hour | Subtle warmth, beautiful | None needed |
| Open shade | Noticeable blue cast | 81A or 81B warming filter |
| Overcast | Strong blue/gray cast | 81B or 81C warming filter |
| Tungsten (3200K) | Heavy orange cast | 80A blue filter (2-stop loss) or avoid |
| Fluorescent | Green/magenta cast | FL-D filter or avoid |
| Flash / strobe | Neutral, works well | None needed |

This sensitivity is both a limitation and a creative tool — the pronounced cold rendering in shade is part of the E100 "look."

## Comparison: E100 vs. Key Competitors

### E100 vs. E100G (Previous Generation)
| Attribute | E100 (2018) | E100G (vintage) |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| White balance | Cooler, more neutral | Slightly warmer |
| Contrast | Higher | Moderate |
| Blue rendering | Pronounced cool-blue | Neutral blue |
| Skin tones | Paler, cooler | Warmer, more forgiving |
| Grain | RMS 8 (equivalent) | RMS 8 |
| Sharpness | Marginally sharper | Excellent |
| Overall character | Modern, editorial, clean | Classic, slightly warmer Ektachrome |

Many photographers who loved E100G find the new E100 slightly "harsher" or "more clinical" — though others prefer its modernized rendering.

### E100 vs. E100VS (Vivid Saturation)
| Attribute | E100 (2018) | E100VS |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Saturation | Moderate | Very high |
| Reds | True, moderate | Pushed, warm |
| Greens | Cool, clean | Hyper-saturated, yellow-green |
| Blues | Clean, cool-cyan | Deep, high-saturation |
| Skin tones | Neutral-cool | Difficult, overly red |
| Best for | General, architecture, editorial | Landscapes, nature, high-impact |
| Availability | Current production | Discontinued since ~2012 |

E100VS is essentially E100 with +25% saturation globally and a warmer white balance. It represents the "Velvia competitor" in Kodak's lineup.

### E100 vs. Fuji Provia 100F
| Attribute | E100 | Provia 100F |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| White balance | 5500K, cool-leaning | 5500K, neutral-to-warm |
| Shadow tint | Cool, neutral | Slightly magenta/warm |
| Blue rendition | Cyan-leaning, strong | True blue, balanced |
| Green rendition | Cool, teal-leaning | Neutral, accurate |
| Skin tones | Cool, editorial | Warm, flattering |
| Contrast | High | Moderate-high |
| Grain | RMS 8 | RMS 8 |
| Dynamic range | ~7–8 stops | ~7–8 stops |

### E100 vs. Fuji Velvia 50/100
| Attribute | E100 | Velvia |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Saturation | Moderate | Extreme |
| Contrast | High | Very high |

## The Return of Ektachrome (2017–2019)

### Timeline of the Comeback
- **2012:** Kodak discontinues all Ektachrome films (E100G, E100VS) amid bankruptcy restructuring
- **January 2017:** Kodak Alaris announces Ektachrome will return, driven by community demand and the film photography resurgence
- **Summer 2017:** Super 8 Ektachrome announced as first format
- **September 2018:** E100 35mm released, ships to retailers globally
- **December 2019:** E100 released in 120 (medium format) and 4x5 sheet film

### Why the Return Mattered
The re-release was culturally significant in the film photography community because:
1. It reversed the narrative of film discontinuation — a film *coming back* was unprecedented in the digital era
2. It validated the film photography resurgence as commercially meaningful, not just nostalgic
3. It filled a vacuum — Fujifilm had discontinued several slide films (Astia, Sensia, Velvia 100 in some markets), and Provia 100F was the only modern slide option
4. It proved Kodak's long-term commitment to film manufacturing
5. The E-6 development infrastructure, which had been declining, received a shot in the arm — labs expanded E-6 capacity to meet demand

### Community Reception
- **Overwhelmingly positive** on release
- Frequent comparisons: "Kodak blue skies" praised; skin tone handling noted as needing warming
- Common critique: Higher price than Provia 100F in some markets
- Consensus: A worthy, modern slide film that stands on its own, not merely a nostalgia product

## Shooting Notes (from community experience)

### Best Subjects for E100
- Architecture (clean lines, blues skies, neutral rendering)
- Landscapes with water (cyan-leaning blues, clean reflections)
- Studio/controlled lighting (strobes at 5500K, perfect color)
- Urban scenes in daylight (modern, crisp rendering)
- Automotive/product photography (clean, clinical accuracy)

### Challenging Subjects for E100
- Portraits in shade (too cool without warming filter)
- Mixed lighting (slide film can't be color-corrected in post like neg or digital)
- Ultra-high-contrast scenes (beyond the ~7-stop dynamic range)
- Indoor available light (tungsten color cast or extremely slow shutter speeds)

### Exposure Wisdom
- "Meter for the highlights, let shadows fall" — the slide film mantra
- Spot metering or incident metering strongly recommended
- Center-weighted auto-exposure on SLRs works surprisingly well if you lock exposure on midtones
- **+1/3 stop overexposure (EI 80)** is popular for slightly more shadow detail without blowing highlights — about 50% of E100 shooters prefer this
- Bracketing ±0.5 stops for critical shots is common practice
