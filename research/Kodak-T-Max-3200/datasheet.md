# Kodak T-Max P3200 — Official Datasheet & Technical Data

> **Film Code:** TMZ  
> **Type:** Panchromatic B&W negative, tabular-grain (T-grain) emulsion  
> **Formats:** 135 (35mm), 120 (medium format)  
> **Introduced:** 1986 (T-Max family), TMZ launched later  
> **Discontinued:** 2012; **Re-introduced:** March 2018  
> **Datasheet:** Kodak publication F-4001 (2018) / F-4016 (2007, broader T-Max family)

---

## True ISO vs. Box Speed

**T-Max P3200 is NOT an ISO 3200 film.** Official Kodak statement (via TJ Mooney, Kodak Alaris, 2018):

> *"Straight out of the box, Kodak T-Max P3200 film is nominally 800 speed, but it is intended to be pushed beyond EI 800 — that's what the 'P' stands for."*

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **True ISO (base)** | ~800–1000 |
| **Box speed labeling** | "3200" (marketing) |
| **DX coding (135)** | ISO 3200 |
| **"P" designation** | Denotes "push" — designed for push processing |
| **Usable EI range** | 400 – 25,000 |
| **Recommended EI range** | 800 – 6400 (defined by Kodak as giving "good results") |

## Characteristic Curves (Summary from F-4016)

Kodak's official datasheet provides characteristic density vs. log exposure curves for development at different EI ratings. Key observations:

- **Toe shape:** Moderate toe (not as long as Tri-X) — shadows drop off more abruptly at higher push levels
- **Linear region:** Good linearity from ~0.3 to ~2.0 density above fb+f
- **Shoulder:** Begins to roll off at density ~2.2–2.4
- **Push effect:** Pushing primarily increases D-max and steepens the curve (higher contrast), rather than meaningfully improving shadow speed
- **At EI 6400+:** Shadow detail severely compressed; effective shadow speed gains are minimal. The increased development primarily boosts midtones and highlights.

### Contrast Index (CI) vs. EI (approximate, D-76 stock)

| EI | Approx. CI | Character |
|---|---|---|
| 800 | 0.50–0.55 | Normal contrast — full tonal range |
| 1600 | 0.58–0.62 | Moderate push — slightly compressed shadows |
| 3200 | 0.65–0.72 | Strong push — high contrast, dramatic look |
| 6400 | 0.72–0.82 | Heavy push — high contrast, blocky shadows |
| 12,500+ | 0.85+ | Extreme — mostly midtones/highlights survive |

## Grain & Resolution

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| **Grain type** | Tabular-grain (T-grain) — flat, plate-like silver halide crystals |
| **RMS granularity** | ~12–14 (at EI 800); increases ~15–18 at EI 3200; ~20+ at EI 6400 |
| **Resolving power** | 125 lines/mm (TOC 1.6:1) / 63 lines/mm (TOC 1000:1) |
| **Perceived grain** | Pronounced but structured — "crisp grain" vs. "clumpy grain" of traditional cubic emulsions |

### T-Grain vs. Traditional Grain

Kodak T-Max uses **tabular-grain technology** (patented 1982, introduced 1986). This differs fundamentally from:

- **Traditional cubic grain** (Tri-X, HP5+): Irregular silver halide crystals — "classic" grain look
- **Epitaxial grain** (Ilford Delta 3200): Core-shell crystal growth, epitaxial overgrowth on cubic cores

**T-grain advantages:** More surface area per crystal → higher sensitivity for a given grain size → finer grain at equivalent speed  
**T-grain aesthetic:** Grain pattern is sharper-edged, more geometric, less "snowy," possibly more visible despite smaller RMS value because of higher acutance

## Spectral Sensitivity

Kodak T-Max films are panchromatic with extended red sensitivity:

- **Panchromatic:** Sensitive to all visible wavelengths (400–700nm)
- **Extended red:** Slightly higher red sensitivity than Tri-X — renders reds slightly lighter
- **No near-IR sensitivity**
- **Blue/UV sensitivity:** Standard panchromatic response

## Reciprocity Failure

| Indicated Time (sec) | Required Exposure (approx.) |
|---|---|
| 1/10,000 – 1 | No correction needed |
| 10 | +1/3 stop |
| 100 | +1 stop |

T-Max films have excellent reciprocity characteristics — significantly better than traditional films like Tri-X.

## Base + Fog

- **fb+f (D-min):** ~0.10–0.15 (35mm), slightly higher for 120
- **D-max:** ~2.2–2.6 (depending on development)
- **Base tint:** Neutral gray (non-tinted base, unlike some other Kodak films)
