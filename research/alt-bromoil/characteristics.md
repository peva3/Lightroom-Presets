# Bromoil — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## Core Aesthetic Signature

The bromoil process, introduced by E.J. Wall in 1907 and refined by C. Welborne Piper, was the dominant pictorialist printing process of the 1910s–1930s. A conventional silver gelatin print is bleached to remove the silver image while hardening the gelatin in proportion to the original density. The print is then inked with a brush using lithographic ink, which adheres only to the hardened (originally dark) areas. The result is a print that looks like an etching, lithograph, or ink drawing.

## Historical Context

**Era:** 1907–1950s

Silver gelatin print → bleached in copper sulfate/potassium bromide → silver removed, gelatin tanned → fixed → soaked in water → inked with lithographic ink using a brush or brayer → ink adheres to hardened (tanned) areas. Multiple inkings can build up density.

## Visual Characteristics

Painterly ink texture resembling brushwork or etching. The inking process creates a unique texture pattern — visible brush strokes and ink stippling. Rich, deep blacks from densely inked areas. Warm brown-black ink tones. The image looks deliberately hand-made. No photographic sharpness — detail is conveyed through ink density, not edge acuity.

## Emotional & Artistic Vocabulary

Deliberate, handcrafted, painterly. Bromoils reject the 'automatic' nature of photography. Each print is individually inked by hand, making every one unique. The process says: this is a handmade object, not a mechanical reproduction.

## Technical Notes for Digital Emulation

This preset is designed to capture the essential visual character of the bromoil process using Lightroom's slider-based toolset. It prioritizes the emotional/aesthetic impact over perfect chemical accuracy, which would require a 3D LUT or DCP camera profile.

### Limitations of Slider-Based Simulation
- True process-specific spectral sensitivity cannot be replicated with sliders
- Chemical stain patterns are simulated through grain and color grading
- Paper/plate texture is approximated through grain settings
