# Community Recipes: Film Soup in Lightroom

Compiled from r/analog, r/Darkroom, r/postprocessing, r/Lightroom, alternative photography forums, and YouTube tutorials. These represent community-sourced methods for achieving the film soup aesthetic digitally.

## Recipe 1: Core Film Soup Emulation

Community consensus: Moderate contrast. Multi-directional color shifts via HSL (desaturate some channels, boost others randomly). Multi-hue color grading (different shadow, midtone, and highlight hues). Very heavy, irregular grain (Amount 45-55, Size 40-50, Roughness 70-85). Subtle vignette. The key is controlled chaos — the preset should suggest damage without overwhelming the image.

## Community Knowledge & Tips

From discussions on r/analog, r/Darkroom, and alternative process forums:

> "The key to any alt-process emulation is restraint. The best presets suggest the process rather than force it. A real cyanotype doesn't look the same on every paper — neither should your preset look the same on every image."

> "Texture (grain) matters more than color for alt-process emulations. The physicality of the print — paper fibers, brush strokes, chemical stains — is what makes these processes feel real. Without texture, you just have a color filter."

> "For warm-toned processes (platinum, palladium, albumen, salt, Vandyke), the shadow warmth is the most important characteristic. A neutral black shadow immediately reads as 'digital B&W with a warm filter.' The warmth has to be in the shadows for it to feel like a real print."

> "The cyanotype and other iron-based processes have a characteristic tonal compression — highlights hang on longer and shadows open up earlier than silver processes. The tone curve matters as much as the color."

## Cross-Process Compatibility

These alternative process emulations work best on:
- RAW files with good exposure latitude
- Images with distinct tonal separation
- Landscapes, still life, and architectural subjects
- Portraits (for warm-toned processes)

They are less effective on:
- High-ISO, noisy digital images (grain compounds)
- Already heavily edited images (color grading conflicts)
- Images without clear subject-background separation
