## Historical Market Transformation

### The Film-to-Digital Transition

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1990 | First consumer digital camera (Dycam Model 1 / Logitech Fotoman) |
| Late 1990s | Consumer digital cameras become widely available |
| 2000 | First camera phones (Sharp, Samsung) |
| 2002 | Cheapest digital cameras at ~$100 USD |
| 2003 | Digital cameras outsell film cameras |
| Jan 2004 | Kodak ends film camera sales in developed world |
| Jan 2006 | Nikon stops all but 2 film camera models |
| 2007 | Konica Minolta exits camera business; AgfaPhoto bankrupt |
| 2012 | Kodak files for bankruptcy |
| Mar 2012 | Peak digital camera sales: ~11 million units/month |
| Mar 2014 | Sales decline to ~3 million/month (~30% of peak) |

### The Smartphone Disruption

Since 2010, standalone digital cameras have faced intense competition from smartphones:
- Smartphones now dominate casual photography
- Most camera phones offer HD video alongside still photography
- Social media integration drives smartphone photography volume
- Standalone camera sales stabilized at ~3 million units/month

### Mirrorless Camera Revolution

As of 2024, mirrorless cameras represent the growth segment in dedicated cameras:
- Compact size and weight vs DSLRs
- Electronic viewfinders with exposure/focus preview
- Silent operation for discreet shooting
- Advanced autofocus tracking for moving subjects
- Growing lens ecosystem
- Replacing DSLRs as the professional standard

## Social Impact of Digital Photography

### Democratization of Photography
- Eliminated cost and delay of film processing
- Home computer editing replaced specialized darkroom equipment
- Camera phones made photography ubiquitous
- Self-service kiosks for smartphone photo printing

### The "Pics or It Didn't Happen" Era
- Photos as social proof of experience
- Identity expression through curated visual aesthetics
- Digital photography as communication tool, not just memory preservation

### Filter Culture
- Filters emulating analog effects (grain, fading, polaroid borders, light leaks)
- Driven by nostalgia for film photography's disappearance
- Social photography prioritizing aesthetic consistency over traditional photographic values
- Massive demand for preset-like one-click editing on mobile

### Digital Preservation Concerns
- Historians warn of "digital dark age" from obsolete media/formats
- Unlike film prints, digital files require active migration between technologies
- Professional photographers need digital preservation strategies

## Photo Editing Market Evolution

### From Chemical to Digital Post-Production
Traditional photography required:
- Dark room equipment
- Hazardous chemicals
- Specialized ventilation
- Washing and drying infrastructure

Digital photography enables:
- Personal computer-based editing
- Non-destructive editing workflows
- Instant preview of adjustments
- Batch processing of hundreds of images
- Cloud-based collaboration and delivery

### Professional Workflow Changes
Digital cameras enabled:
- Immediacy: instant image review and deletion
- Faster workflow: digital color management, file handling
- Higher image quantity: longer sessions without film changes
- Precision: deterministic, reproducible processing
- Digital manipulation: complex edits not possible with film

## Key Market Statistics

### Camera Sales Trajectory
- **2003**: Digital surpasses film (inflection point)
- **2012**: Peak at 11M units/month
- **2014**: ~3M units/month (70% decline from peak)
- **Post-2014**: Stabilized around 3M/month

### Film Industry Collapse
- Kodak: From photography giant to 2012 bankruptcy
- Fuji: Shifted from film to broader imaging/materials
- Agfa: Film division bankrupt by 2005
- Konica Minolta: Exited camera business entirely (2006)

### Digital Growth Areas
- Digital photo frames
- Canvas prints
- Online photo sharing (Flickr, Picasa, PhotoBucket)
- Social media photography (Instagram, Snapchat)
- Smartphone photo printing services

## Implications for the Preset Market

1. **Massive addressable market**: Billions of smartphone photos taken daily, creating demand for one-tap editing solutions
2. **Nostalgia premium**: Film simulation presets command higher prices due to emotional connection to analog aesthetics
3. **Platform fragmentation**: Need for presets compatible across Lightroom Classic, Lightroom CC, Lightroom Mobile, and third-party RAW editors
4. **Professional workflow**: Batch editing presets remain essential for high-volume shooters (wedding, event, real estate)
5. **Mobile-first editing**: Growing demand for presets designed specifically for phone-edited images (different color science, smaller files)
6. **Mirrorless camera market**: New camera profiles and color science means presets must be calibrated for modern sensors
