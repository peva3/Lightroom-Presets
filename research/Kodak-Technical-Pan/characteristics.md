# Kodak Technical Pan — Film Characteristics for Lightroom Presets

## Overview
Kodak Technical Pan (SO-115) was the highest-resolution black-and-white film ever commercially produced. Originally developed for scientific and technical applications (hence the name), it was adopted by photographers seeking maximum detail. NASA used it for lunar mapping and satellite reconnaissance. Discontinued in 2004. This document serves as a reference for creating a Lightroom preset that emulates Technical Pan.

## Key Characteristics
- **ISO**: 25 (with Technidol developer); can range from 16-64 depending on developer
- **Grain**: Essentially nonexistent — resolving power exceeds 400 lp/mm
- **Sharpness**: The highest ever — capable of resolving individual emulsion grains on glass plates
- **Contrast**: Extremely high by default (gamma > 2.0) — requires special low-contrast developers (Technidol, POTA, C-41) for pictorial use
- **Spectral Sensitivity**: Extended red (super-panchromatic) — sensitive into near-infrared
- **Tonal Range**: Very compressed unless used with low-contrast developers
- **Exposure Latitude**: Very narrow — demands precise exposure

## Historical Context
- Originally Kodak High Contrast Copy Film, reformulated as SO-115 Technical Pan
- Used by NASA for Apollo lunar mapping and Skylab solar photography
- Adopted by fine-art photographers (notably for extreme enlargements) despite its difficulty
- Special Technidol developer made pictorial contrast possible — without it, the film is essentially lithographic
- Discontinued 2004; no direct replacement exists
- Rollei ATP (Advanced Technical Pan) is the modern successor (Rollei/Maco)

## Visual Signature
- When properly developed: ultra-smooth tonality with surreal sharpness
- Resolves detail invisible to other films — individual leaves on distant trees, fabric textures at extreme range
- Extended red sensitivity lightens skin and darkens blue skies dramatically
- Grain is completely invisible — the image appears "liquid smooth"
- Can look unnaturally sharp and "digital" if over-sharpened in post
- The extended red sensitivity creates a distinctive near-IR look in landscapes
