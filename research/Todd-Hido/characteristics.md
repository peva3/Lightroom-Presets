# Todd Hido

## Biography
- **Name**: Todd Hido (born 1968)
- **Nationality**: American
- **Era**: 1990s–present

## Philosophy
- "I've always been drawn to the mysterious, the hidden, the thing that's just out of reach."
- Suburban America at night as psychological landscape
- Houses as portraits of their inhabitants without showing people
- Attracted to bad weather: "The worse the weather, the better the picture."
- Narrative absence: images suggest stories the viewer must complete

## Technical Approach
- **Gear**: Pentax 67 medium format, various 35mm, Holga, digital
- **Film**: Kodak Portra 400, 800, various color negative films, some expired
- **Shooting**: Through car windows (his own windshield), rain, fog, at night
- **Lighting**: Available light only — streetlights, porch lights, TV glow through windows
- **Printing**: C-prints, later inkjet; known for dark, moody prints with subtle detail in shadows

## Visual Signature
- **Cool blue/magenta night cast**: The streetlight spectrum — sodium vapor (warm) mixed with fluorescent/LED (cool) creates distinctive magenta-blue shadows
- **Homes at night**: Isolated suburban houses, windows glowing, surrounded by darkness
- **Through-windshield haze**: Rain on glass, condensation, blurring — the car as camera
- **Fog and mist**: Negative clarity and atmospheric softness that suggests cold night air
- **Underexposure as aesthetic**: Dark but never black; shadow detail is preserved in the deep blue
- **Lonely beauty**: Melancholic but never ugly — there's warmth in those glowing windows

## Iconic Images
- *House Hunting* (2001)
- *Roaming* (2004)
- *Excerpts from Silver Meadows* (2013)

## Preset Design Notes
- Underexposed base with significant shadow lift — the night paradox: dark but visible
- Blue/magenta shadow cast (Hue 230) with cool global balance shift
- Green desaturation (suburban lawns at night are not green)
- Negative dehaze for atmospheric fog/haze
- Negative Clarity for the through-glass softness
- Slight magenta saturation boost (the streetlight magenta)
- Blending at 75 for cinematic color separation
