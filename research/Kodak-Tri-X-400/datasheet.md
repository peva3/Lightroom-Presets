# Kodak Tri-X 400 — Official Datasheet

> **Source:** Kodak Professional technical publication F-4017 (Tri-X Pan / 400TX)
> **Format:** 35mm, 120, 4×5 sheet film
> **Product code:** 400TX / TX 400

---

## Overview

Kodak Professional Tri-X 400 (400TX) is a high-speed panchromatic black-and-white negative film designed for general use, especially photojournalism, documentary, and street photography. It features a traditional cubic-grain emulsion with a distinctive "salt-and-pepper" grain structure praised for its character, sharpness, and immense push-processing latitude.

- **ISO:** 400 (nominal box speed)
- **Film base:** Gray acetate (35mm), 5-mil (120), 7-mil (4×5)
- **Spectral sensitivity:** Panchromatic with extended red sensitivity
- **Grain type:** Traditional cubic (non-tabular)
- **Antihalation:** Gray dye in 35mm; remjet-free; 120 base has anti-halation undercoat

## Sensitometric Data (Characteristic Curves)

### Development: Kodak D-76 at 20°C (68°F)

| Development Time | Gamma | Contrast Index | Effective Speed |
|-----------------|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| 6 min | 0.55 | 0.42 | ~250 |
| 6.75 min | 0.62 | 0.48 | ~320 |
| 8 min | 0.70 | 0.55 | ~400 (box) |
| 9.75 min | 0.80 | 0.62 | ~400–500 |
| 11 min | 0.90 | 0.70 | ~500 |

The characteristic curve for Tri-X is notable for its **long, straight-line section** with a gentle shoulder in the highlight region. This gives Tri-X its signature ability to retain highlight detail even when overexposed by 2–3 stops, while maintaining deep, rich blacks.

### Spectral Sensitivity (Density at 1.0 above D-min, equal-energy source)

| Wavelength (nm) | Relative Log Sensitivity |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| 400 | 0.85 |
| 450 | 0.95 |
| 500 | 0.98 |
| 550 | 1.00 (peak) |
| 600 | 0.92 |
| 650 | 0.72 |
| 700 | 0.15 |

The extended red sensitivity (up to ~680nm) gives Tri-X its characteristic rendering of skin tones — slightly lightened reds/oranges compared to orthopanchromatic films, which translates to flattering caucasian skin tone rendering (about 1/3 stop brighter than visual perception).

## Granularity

**RMS Granularity (diffuse): 17**
(Measured at net density 1.0, 48-micrometer aperture)

This is relatively coarse by modern standards (T-MAX 400 is RMS 10), but the grain structure is what gives Tri-X its legendary look:
- "Salt-and-pepper" distribution — sharp-edged grains with distinct spacing
- Grain clumps in mid-tones, creating the famous "peppery" texture
- At box speed in D-76: clearly visible grain in 8×10" from 35mm
- Pushed to 1600: grain becomes pronounced and aggressive — highly prized

| Contrast Ratio | Resolution |

| 1000:1 (high) | 100 lines/mm |

Note: Real-world resolution depends heavily on developer choice. HC-110 dilutions and Rodinal dramatically increase perceived acutance (edge sharpness) at the cost of slightly lower measured resolution.

### Extreme Push (Community Reports)

- **EI 3200** (HC-110 Dil B, ~14-16 min): High contrast, heavy grain, usable shadow detail if scene contrast is low
- **EI 6400** (HC-110 Dil A, ~18-22 min): "Desperation push" — massive grain, near-zero shadow detail, extreme contrast
- **EI 12800** (HC-110 1:64 from syrup, 120 min stand development): Documented on r/analog — "grain is there. contrast is, too. but it works." (u/provia, 2019)
- **Semi-stand with HC-110 1:63 (44 min)**: Reported by u/phphoto on r/analog (2013) — "incredible contrast and grain"

## Key Historical Notes

- Introduced as sheet film in the 1940s; 35mm format in 1954
- Reformulated multiple times; current "400TX" replaces the older "Tri-X Pan" (TX)
- The current emulsion is thinner and has slightly finer grain than the pre-2007 version
- Used for virtually every major conflict from Vietnam through Iraq by photojournalists
- Famous users: Henri Cartier-Bresson, Sebastião Salgado, Daido Moriyama, Don McCullin, Mary Ellen Mark, Bruce Davidson, Garry Winogrand, Josef Koudelka

## 35mm vs 120 vs 4×5 Rendering Differences

| Format | Grain Visibility (8×10 print) | Perceived Sharpness | Tonal Smoothness |
|--------|------------------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| 35mm | Very pronounced — signature look | Acceptable-High | Gradated |
| 120 | Visible but refined | High-Very High | Smooth |
| 4×5 | Nearly invisible | Maximum | Very smooth |

The "Tri-X look" is most identifiable in 35mm format where the grain delivers its iconic texture. In medium format, Tri-X becomes a much cleaner film while retaining the characteristic contrast curve. In 4×5, it behaves almost like a medium-speed film with beautiful open shadows.
