# Cinestill 800T — Technical Datasheet

## Origin & Base Film

Cinestill 800T is **not** a ground-up film formulation. It is **repurposed Kodak Vision3 500T** motion picture stock (5219/7219) — the industry-standard tungsten-balanced cinema negative that has been used on countless Hollywood films.

Cinestill (the company) purchases bulk rolls of Kodak Vision3 500T, removes the **remjet backing layer** in a proprietary pre-bath process, then repackages and resells it as a C-41 still photography film. The "800" in the name reflects the practical push that results from cross-processing motion picture ECN-2 stock in standard C-41 chemistry, which yields approximately 1/3 to 2/3 stop of additional effective speed.

## Remjet Removal & Its Effects

### What is Remjet?

Remjet (removable jet-black) is a carbon-based anti-halation backing applied to the back of motion picture film. In cinema cameras, it serves multiple purposes:
- **Anti-halation**: Absorbs light that passes through the emulsion, preventing it from reflecting off the pressure plate back into the film layers
- **Anti-static**: Reduces static discharge during high-speed film transport (24+ fps)
- **Lubrication**: Protects film as it runs through cinema camera movements

### The Removal Process

Cinestill removes the remjet layer before packaging using a proprietary pre-soak. This is necessary because remjet dissolves in ECN-2 developer but **contaminates standard C-41 chemistry** — it would destroy minilab processing machines and home development chemicals alike.

### Consequences of Remjet Removal

1. **Red Halation**: The single most iconic characteristic of Cinestill 800T. Without the remjet anti-halation backing, light from bright sources (streetlights, neon signs, car headlights) penetrates through the emulsion, reflects off the film base, and re-exposes the red-sensitive layer from behind. This creates the signature **red/orange halation glow** around specular highlights. It is most prominent on the red layer because red light penetrates deepest into the emulsion.

2. **Increased Base Fog / Lower Contrast**: Remjet also serves as an anti-halation undercoat. Without it, some light pipes through the film base even in mid-tones, slightly lifting shadow densities. This contributes to the "glowy," cinematic look.

3. **C-41 Cross-Processing Color Shift**: ECN-2 and C-41 have different color developers. Cross-processing shifts the color palette — typically intensifying blues and cyans while warming certain skin tones unpredictably.

4. **Light Piping**: 35mm canisters allow light to travel along the film base into the first few frames. Cinestill recommends loading/unloading in subdued light and warns that the first 1–2 frames will likely show edge fogging.

## Halation: The Science

The red halation effect is a **physical artifact**, not an emulsion characteristic. Here's what happens:

1. Bright point source light enters the emulsion at the focal plane
2. Light passes through all dye layers (blue-sensitive, yellow filter, green-sensitive, red-sensitive)
3. Reaches the film base and reflects off the back surface
4. Reflected light re-passes through the red-sensitive layer from behind
5. Red-sensitive layer records this secondary exposure as an unfocused, diffuse glow
6. Because the light traveled through the base and back, the glow is larger than the original source — this is **halation**, not bloom

In normal Vision3 500T, the remjet absorbs this light before it can reflect. In Cinestill 800T, nothing stops it.

The effect varies by format:
- **35mm**: Strong halation; thinner base = shorter light path = tighter, more defined halos
- **120 medium format**: Even stronger halation; larger film area means more internal reflection paths

## Shooting Recommendations (from Community)

From Marco Manuzzi's experience (35mmc, 2022):

| Setting | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Metering mode | Center-weighted average |
| Rated ISO | 1600–2000 (push +2) or 3200 (push +3) |
| Exposure priority | Aperture priority |
| Camera WB (digital reference) | 2800–3200K |
| Daylight use | Requires 85B warming filter (otherwise very cool/blue) |
| Light sources | Best with mixed artificial lighting, neon, sodium vapor, LED |
| Loading | In subdued light; expect first 1–2 frames fogged |

### Push Processing Notes

Many shooters rate Cinestill 800T at 1600 or 3200 and push-process +1 to +3 stops. Cinestill is famously tolerant of underexposure due to Vision3's high latitude. Pushed results have:
- Elevated grain
- Increased contrast
- More saturated reds/cyans
- Exaggerated halation (more light energy = more reflection)

## Trademark Controversy (2023)

In October 2023, Cinestill sent cease-and-desist letters to retailers selling "Reflx Lab 800 Tungsten" (another Vision3-based film with remjet removed). Cinestill holds US trademarks for "CINESTILL," "800T," and claims common-law rights to "800TUNGSTEN." The film community largely criticized Cinestill's action, noting that "800T" is simply an ISO + white-balance descriptor that predates Cinestill (see Kodak's own 500T, 250D naming convention). The controversy was widely discussed on r/AnalogCommunity and covered by 35mmc and PetaPixel.
