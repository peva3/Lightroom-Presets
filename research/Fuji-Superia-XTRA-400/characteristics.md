# Fuji Superia X-TRA 400 — Full Aesthetic Characteristics Breakdown

## Overview

Fuji Superia X-TRA 400 is a consumer-grade ISO 400 color negative film introduced in 1998 (replacing Super G Plus 400), updated in 2003 and 2006, and discontinued globally in April 2024. It was sold as the cheap 4-pack drugstore film — available at Walmart, CVS, Walgreens — and became a beloved staple of street photography, everyday snapshots, and budget-conscious film shooters. Its discontinuation marks the end of Fujifilm's in-house consumer color negative film production.

**Community nicknames**: "The cheap 4-pack drugstore film," "The cool Fuji look," "Xtra," "Gritty street staple"

---

## 1. Core Aesthetic Signature

### Primary Color Cast
**COOL**. This is the single most defining characteristic of Superia X-TRA 400 vs. any Kodak film. Where Kodak runs warm (gold/yellow), Fuji runs cool (green/magenta). This is fundamentally different from Portra, Gold, UltraMax, and every other Kodak stock.

The "Fuji look" is instantly recognizable: cool-toned with a slight green/magenta bias.

---

## 2. Green Shadow Cast (The Defining Trait)

Superia X-TRA 400 is famous — and infamous — for its greenish shadow rendering. This is **the** signature characteristic that sets it apart from every other consumer film.

### How It Manifests
- **Deep shadows**: Murky green-gray, not true black. Shadows never go to neutral black — there's always a greenish undercurrent.
- **Mid-shadow transition**: As tones transition from midtones into shadows, a green tint creeps in.
- **Underexposed areas**: More pronounced green cast. Intentional underexposure amplifies it.
- **Overcast/cloudy conditions**: The green shadow cast is most visible in flat light.

### Technical Origin
- The 4th Color Layer (cyan-sensitive) technology interacts with the magenta-forming green-sensitive layer during dye coupling.
- The "New Super Uniform Fine Grain Technology" (flat hexagonal grains) affects dye cloud formation differently than traditional cubic grains.
- Consumer-grade couplers in the magenta layer leave a slight green residual in low-density (shadow) areas.

### Community Descriptions
- "Murky greenish shadows" — most common descriptor
- "Green-gray underworld"
- "Swampy blacks" (said affectionately)
- "That Fuji green cast in the blacks"
- "Shadows that look like they're underwater"

### Comparison
- **Kodak UltraMax 400**: Teal/cyan shadows (cooler but more blue, less green)
- **Kodak Gold 200**: Warm brown shadows (opposite direction)
- **Kodak Portra 400**: Neutral to slightly cool shadows (much cleaner)
- **Fuji Pro 400H**: Similar green cast but more refined, less murky (professional couplers)

---

## 3. Magenta-Red Push (The Punch)

Superia X-TRA 400 has a distinctive red rendering that leans magenta. Reds are prominent, punchy, and slightly shifted toward the magenta/crimson end of the spectrum rather than true red or orange-red.

### How It Manifests
- **Pure reds**: Shift toward magenta-crimson. Red stop signs, clothing, flowers look slightly "cooler" red than Kodak's warm/orange red.
- **Skin tones containing red**: Slight magenta cast — can look either flattering (rosy cheeks) or slightly unnatural depending on subject.
- **Magenta/pink tones**: Amplified. Pink flowers, neon signs, and magenta clothing really pop.
- **Red in mixed light**: The 4th color layer maintains red accuracy under fluorescent lighting where Kodak films would shift yellow-green.

### Community Descriptions
- "Punchy magenta-reds" — this exact phrase appears repeatedly
- "That Fuji red pop"
- "Reds that bite"
- "Crimson shadows"

### Comparison
- **Kodak UltraMax 400**: Reds lean orange-warm
- **Kodak Gold 200**: Reds are warm, slightly amber
- **Kodak Ektar 100**: Reds are saturated but true red (neutral hue)
- **Fuji Pro 400H**: Similar magenta-red but more muted, pastel-like

---

## 4. Greens

Superia's green rendering is lush but not pushed toward yellow (Kodak warmth). Foliage appears "true green" — slightly cool, natural, without the golden warmth Kodak imparts.

- **Foliage**: Authentic, pleasant green. One reviewer: "great green tones"
- **Grass/trees**: Natural rendering without yellow bias
- **Fluorescent-lit greens**: Handled well by 4th color layer
- **Green cast interference**: The shadow green cast can bleed into dark green objects, making them appear extra green

### Scott Tucker's Description
"Green greens, slight greens in the shadows" — the Superia aesthetic in one phrase.

---

## 5. Blues

- **Sky blues**: Lighter, more pastel blue than Kodak's deeper sky rendering. The consumer-grade dye set renders blue skies with a slight washed quality.
- **Blue in shadows**: Infected by the green shadow cast — blue shadows become teal-green.
- **Deep blues**: Maintain saturation well. Navy, indigo, and dark blue clothing render richly.

### Fuji X Weekly Note
The X-Trans V sensor "renders blues just a little deeper" on Classic Negative, so the Color Chrome FX Blue setting was reduced from Strong (X-Trans IV) to Weak (X-Trans V) to match actual Superia film.

---

## 6. Skin Tones

- **Caucasian skin**: Neutral to slightly cool. Less yellow than Kodak. Some describe it as "a bit ashy" or "pale."
- **Darker skin tones**: Cool bias can work well — less red/orange exaggeration than Kodak.
- **Flash-lit skin**: Good results. Superia was designed with consumer flash photography in mind.
- **Overexposed skin**: Pastel and smooth. At +1 stop, skin tones improve significantly.
- **Community rating**: "Excellent Skin Color Reproduction" per Fujifilm datasheet, but community feedback is mixed. More versatile than flattering — works well for street/environmental portraits, less ideal for beauty/fashion.

### r/AnalogCommunity Sentiment
Superia skin tones are polarizing. Some love the cool, editorial look; others find it too clinical compared to Portra's warmth. The green shadow cast can make skin in shadow areas look slightly sickly.

---

## 7. Yellows & Oranges

- **Yellows**: Accurate, not particularly warm. Yellow objects render true without the golden-amber cast of Kodak.
- **Oranges**: More toward red-orange than yellow-orange. The magenta bias pulls orange slightly toward red.
- **Golden hour**: Less amplified than Kodak. The film's cool bias counteracts golden hour warmth for a more balanced rendering.
- **Autumn foliage**: Works well — reds and oranges pop, but green shadows keep the overall image cool.

---

## 8. Whites & Highlights

- **Whites**: Clean, neutral to slightly cool. Not the warm cream of Kodak films.
- **Highlight roll-off**: Moderate — consumer film compression. Strong light sources blow out with a slight cool fringe.
- **White balance baseline**: Daylight-balanced at ~5500K, but renders cooler than Kodak daylight films at equivalent color temp.

---

## 9. Contrast & Tonality

### Overall Contrast: Medium to Medium-High
Consumer-grade punch — stronger than professional films (Portra) but not as aggressive as slide film.

### S-Curve Character
- **Shadows**: Compress quickly. Not much detail in deep shadows. The green cast makes shadow compression more noticeable.
- **Midtones**: Good separation. Strong midtone contrast gives images their "pop."
- **Highlights**: Moderate roll-off. Detail maintained in bright areas but not the smooth, graceful compression of professional films.

### Dynamic Range
- **Estimated**: 11–13 stops (typical C-41 consumer film)
- **Less than Portra 400**: Portra has ~15 stops of usable latitude
- **More than E-6 slide film**: Significantly wider than any reversal film

### The "Consumer Contrast" Look
Superia has what the community calls "consumer contrast" — punchy enough to look good straight from the minilab, with enough snap to work without post-processing. Not as flat as professional negative film (which was designed for post-processing flexibility), not as contrasty as slide film.

---

## 10. Grain

### Grain Level: Moderate (Fine for a 400-speed consumer film)
Fujifilm's hexagonal grain technology (flat tabular grains) produced grain that was competitive with Kodak T-GRAIN at the consumer level.

### Grain Character
- **Type**: Fine-medium, slightly irregular
- **Most visible in**: Skies, midtones, underexposed areas
- **Character**: Pleasantly textured, not harsh. Has a "gritty but not ugly" quality.

### Grain Comparison
- **Finer than**: Old pre-2006 Superia (cubic grain), expired consumer films
- **Coarser than**: Portra 400 (Kodak T-GRAIN), Ektar 100, Pro 400H
- **Similar to**: Kodak UltraMax 400 (slightly finer), Lomography 400
- **Half-frame**: Noticeably amplified — the gritty street aesthetic amplified

### Fujifilm Datasheet
RMS Granularity: 4 (micro-densitometer, 48µm aperture, density 1.0 above D-min). This is competitive for consumer ISO 400 film of its era.

---

## 11. Exposure Behavior

### Box Speed (ISO 400)
- Punchy, saturated, cool-toned
- Visible grain
- Green shadows present
- Best for: Sunny to partly cloudy daylight

### Overexposure +1 Stop (EI 200)
- Recommended by many shooters
- Shadows open up, green cast reduces
- Colors remain stable
- Grain reduces slightly
- Skin tones improve
- **Community favorite setting** for expired stock

### Overexposure +2 Stops (EI 100)
- Pastel, softer look
- Shadow green cast mostly gone
- Colors desaturate slightly
- Still usable — good for high-contrast scenes

### Underexposure -1 Stop (EI 800)
- Green shadow cast intensifies dramatically
- Grain becomes very prominent
- Colors desaturate
- Murky, gritty aesthetic (embraced by some for street photography)
- Not recommended for general use

### Push Processing
| Rating | Effective ISO | Stops | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pull -2 | 100 | -2 | Softer, reduced contrast |
| Pull -1 | 200 | -1 | Smoother, slightly warmer |
| Box | 400 | 0 | Standard |
| Push +1 | 800 | +1 | Noticeable grain, cooler shift |
| Push +2 | 1600 | +2 | Heavy grain, quality loss |
| Push +3 | 3200 | +3 | Extreme grain, not recommended |

## 12. Lighting Conditions Performance

### Direct Sunlight (Daylight ~5500K)
**Excellent**. This is Superia's sweet spot. Cool tones balance bright sun. Reds pop. Greens render lush.

### Golden Hour
**Good but different from Kodak**. The film's cool bias counteracts some golden hour warmth. Reds and oranges pop but overall image stays cooler than Kodak equivalents. Some shooters prefer this balanced golden hour rendering.

### Overcast / Shade
**Distinctive**. The green shadow cast is most visible. Murky, moody aesthetic. "Gritty" look that street photographers love. Some shooters recommend +1 stop exposure compensation in overcast.

### Flash
**Excellent**. Superia was designed with consumer flash photography in mind. 4th color layer technology was marketed for "beautiful colors under any light." Flash-lit scenes have good skin tones and balanced color.

### Fluorescent / Mixed Light
**Excellent (for its era)**. The 4th Color Layer specifically targets fluorescent light correction. No green cast from fluorescent tubes (the irony: the film adds green to shadows naturally, but removes the fluorescent green cast from ambient light). This was Superia's major marketing advantage over Kodak consumer films.

### Tungsten / Indoor
**Amber cast** (as expected for daylight film without 80A filter). Warm amber-orange rendering. Can be corrected in scanning or embraced for nostalgic effect.

### Night / Low Light
**Usable**. Grain increases but the cool color palette works well with artificial lighting. Good for neon signs and city lights — the cool cast creates interesting color contrast with warm artificial sources.

---

## 13. Scanning & Post-Processing Characteristics

### The "Noritsu vs. Frontier" Effect
Scanning heavily influences Superia's final look:
- **Noritsu scans**: Warmer, more contrast, slightly less green in shadows
- **Frontier scans**: Cooler, greener shadows, more pronounced "Fuji look"
- **Flatbed / DSLR scan**: Most neutral; the true film character emerges

### Post-Processing Masking Color
Per Fujifilm datasheet: "The new SUPERIA X-TRA 400 has slight reddish tint in comparison with the current SUPERIA X-TRA 400." This refers to the unprocessed film base color — relevant for home scanning and inversion.

## 14. "Vibe" & Cultural Context

### Positioning
The accessible, affordable, everywhere film. The film your mom bought at Walmart. The film in every disposable camera (Fuji QuickSnap). The film that didn't require thinking about — just load, shoot, drop off at the drugstore.

### Cultural Associations
- **1998–2010**: Peak consumer film era. Family vacations, school events, birthday parties.
- **2010–2020**: Resurgence in film photography. Adopted by street photographers for its distinctive cool look. Became a "look" rather than just "cheap film."
- **2020–2024**: Discontinuation era. Price gouging. Hoarding. Replaced by Kodak-made "Fujifilm 400."
- **Post-2024**: Legend status. The Fuji look lives on through emulation and expired stock.

### Iconic Use Cases
- **Street photography**: The gritty, cool aesthetic perfectly matches urban environments. Green shadows + concrete = classic street look.
- **Travel snapshots**: Versatile enough for any travel situation.
- **Point-and-shoot culture**: The perfect film for Olympus Stylus Epic, Yashica T4, Contax T2/T3.
- **Disposable camera aesthetic**: Fuji QuickSnap loaded with Superia defined a generation of casual photography.

### The "Fuji 400" Replacement Controversy
When Fujifilm discontinued Superia X-TRA 400 in 2024, they launched "Fujifilm 400" — manufactured by Kodak in the USA. This film produces **warmer, Kodak-like tones** and does NOT replicate the Superia look. The community largely rejected it as a true replacement. As metergeist.com notes: "This discontinuation effectively marks the end of Fujifilm's consumer color negative film production."

### Related Discontinued Fuji Films
- **Fuji Superia 100**: Discontinued ~2009
- **Fuji Superia 200**: Discontinued
- **Fuji Superia 800**: Discontinued
- **Fuji Superia 1600**: Discontinued earlier
- **Fuji Pro 400H**: Discontinued 2021 (professional version)
- **Fuji Pro 160NS**: Discontinued

The entire Superia and Pro color negative lineup is now gone. Only Fuji's slide film (Velvia, Provia) and Instax remain in production.

## 15. Strengths / Best Use Cases

- **Street photography**: The definitive consumer street film. Cool tones + green shadows + gritty grain = iconic street look.
- **Daylight photography**: Excellent in sun. Cool tones balance harsh midday light.
- **Mixed lighting environments**: The 4th color layer handles fluorescent and mixed lighting better than Kodak equivalents.
- **Flash photography**: Designed for it. Natural-looking flash results.
- **Budget shooting**: Was the cheapest color film available. Every frame didn't feel precious.
- **Creating a "1990s–2000s snapshot" aesthetic**: The quintessential consumer film look of the pre-digital era.
- **Green-heavy scenes**: Forests, gardens, parks look fantastic with Superia's cool green rendering.
- **Overcast/moody scenes**: The green shadow cast creates an intentional mood that other films can't replicate.

---

## 16. Weaknesses / Limitations

- **Skin tones**: Less flattering than Portra or Gold. Cool, sometimes ashy. The green shadow cast can make shadow-side skin look sickly.
- **Shadow muddiness**: The signature green cast is not always desired. Can look "dirty" for clean/commercial work.
- **Grain**: Noticeable in 35mm. Not as fine as professional alternatives.
- **Latitude**: Less forgiving than professional films. Requires more careful exposure.
- **Consistency**: Consumer-grade manufacturing tolerances meant batch variation existed.
- **No 120 format** (after ~2013): No medium format option for the later years.
- **Discontinued**: Now expensive and increasingly expired.

## 18. Summary: The Superia X-TRA 400 "Formula"

```
Cool overall color cast (the anti-Kodak)
Green-gray shadow rendering (signature trait)
Magenta-biased reds, punchy and prominent
Cool green foliage (not yellow-green)
Light, slightly washed blue skies
Medium-high consumer contrast with S-curve tonality
Moderate grain, fine for 400-speed consumer film
4th color layer for mixed-light color accuracy
Versatile but thrives in daylight and flash
Gritty, authentic, unpretentious — the people's film
```

This creates the characteristic **cool, punchy, green-shadowed consumer film look** that has made Superia X-TRA 400 one of the most beloved — and now most missed — film stocks in analog photography history.
