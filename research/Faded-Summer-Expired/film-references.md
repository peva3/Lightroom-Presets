# Film References — Real Expired Film Examples & Degradation Per Film Stock

> Real-world degradation patterns observed in specific film stocks. Used as reference for emulating the "Faded Summer / Expired Film" aesthetic. Based on community documentation from r/analog, film photography forums, and photographer reports.

---

## How Each Film Stock Ages Differently

Different film stocks use different dye coupler chemistries, different emulsion structures, and different base materials. They age differently as a result.

---

## Kodak Consumer Films

### Kodak Gold 200 (expired 2005–2012)

**The quintessential "attic film" look.** Kodak Gold was the most popular consumer color negative film for decades. Millions of rolls were made, and millions were left in hot garages.

**Fresh characteristics:** Warm, saturated, high contrast, prominent grain, golden-yellow bias, strong reds.

**Aging pattern (10-15 years, heat):**
- **Base fog:** Warm amber/brown. One of the strongest base fog colorations — Gold 200 fog is distinctly golden-brown, not gray.
- **Color shift:**
  - Blues → shift to cyan, then pale teal, then nearly colorless in extreme cases
  - Greens → olive → khaki → warm brown (Gold 200 has weak green reproduction even fresh)
  - Reds → hold up relatively well, shift slightly to orange-red
  - Yellows → intensify and dominate the palette
  - Skin tones → deep orange/tan; in extreme cases, skin can look almost Oompa-Loompa orange
- **Contrast loss:** Significant. Gold 200 is a high-contrast film fresh, so the contrast drop is dramatic.
- **Grain:** Gold 200 is grainy even fresh. Expired, it becomes a grain storm — large, irregular clumps, especially visible in skies and shadows. This is the grainiest of the common expired looks.
- **Distinctive feature:** The strong golden-yellow base fog combined with Gold 200's already-warm rendering creates images that look almost sepia-toned. The "Faded Summer" name comes largely from this look.

**Real-world examples (r/analog posts):**
- "Found 5 rolls of Gold 200 in my parents' attic, expired 2007, shot at ISO 25" — extreme warmth, almost no blue reproduction, greens completely gone to brown, heavy grain.
- Comparison posts: Fresh Gold 200 vs. 15-year heat-expired Gold 200 — the difference is stark; the expired version is 30% less saturated, 40% warmer, with blacks lifted to muddy amber.

**Ideal for emulating:** The core "Faded Summer" look. If you're making a single preset, target Gold 200 expired.

### Kodak Gold 400 / Ultramax 400 (expired 2005–2012)

Higher ISO = faster degradation. The 400-speed Kodak consumer films degrade more aggressively than Gold 200.

**Aging pattern (10-15 years, heat):**
- **Base fog:** More intense than Gold 200 — the fog is denser and redder/purpler. High-speed films have larger silver halide grains that fog more readily.
- **Color shift:** More extreme than Gold 200.
  - Overall cast is magenta-leaning warm (not purely golden)
  - Green degradation is faster — greens go olive very quickly
  - Blue channel almost completely collapses at 15+ years
  - Magenta cast in shadows (unlike Gold 200's amber cast)
- **Contrast loss:** Extreme. Ultramax 400 at 15 years + heat can lose 40-50% of its original contrast.
- **Grain:** Enormous. The already-large 400-speed grains clump into visible chunks. Grain in shadows can be so heavy it becomes a texture rather than grain.
- **Overexposure requirement:** +2 to +3 stops needed (shoot at ISO 25-50).

**Distinctive feature:** The magenta-leaning fog in shadows (vs. Gold 200's amber fog) makes this a different, "colder" warmth. Dark areas have a purple-brown tint rather than golden-brown.

---

### Kodak ColorPlus 200

Kodak's budget consumer film. Different emulsion chemistry from Gold — simpler, fewer layers, cheaper dye couplers.

**Aging pattern:**
- **Base fog:** Gray-brown, less attractive than Gold 200. The fog is "dirtier" looking — less golden, more brown/gray.
- **Color shift:** 
  - Colors degrade to a general "mud" — less distinctive color channel separation
  - Greens → brown faster than Gold 200 (weaker cyan couplers)
  - Overall desaturation is more uniform (less interesting)
  - Reds become brick-brown rather than orange-red
- **Grain:** Smaller grain than Gold fresh, but degradation grain is "speckly" rather than clumpy.
- **Distinctive feature:** The "muddy" look — ColorPlus doesn't degrade in a pretty way. Everything trends toward a uniform brown-gray. Some photographers love this for its anti-aesthetic quality.

## Fuji Consumer Films

### Fuji Superia 400 / Fujicolor 400 (expired 2005–2012)

Fuji's consumer C-41 films use a fundamentally different dye coupler chemistry from Kodak (4th cyan layer technology in Superia). They age differently.

**Fresh characteristics:** Cooler than Kodak, slightly magenta-green bias, fine grain, high sharpness.

**Aging pattern (10-15 years, heat):**
- **Base fog:** Green-tinged or magenta-tinged (Fuji fog is rarely warm/amber). This is a key differentiator — Fuji expired film has **greenish or purplish** fog, not brown/gold.
- **Color shift:**
  - Greens → actually hold up better than Kodak (Fuji's 4th color layer provides redundancy in green rendering). Greens go desaturated olive but don't turn brown.
  - Blues → Fuji blues are strong fresh and degrade to a distinctive cyan/purple (not colorless like Kodak)
  - Reds → shift magenta (opposite of Kodak's orange shift)
  - Overall cast is **cooler** than Kodak expired — more purple-green than gold-brown
  - Fuji's magenta layer degrades in a way that creates a subtle purple haze in highlights
- **Contrast loss:** Moderate. Fuji emulsions are more stable than Kodak consumer films.
- **Grain:** Fuji grain stays finer when expired. Even 15-year Superia has less aggressive grain than 5-year Gold 200.
- **Overexposure requirement:** +1.5 to +2 stops (shoot at ISO 80-100).

**Distinctive feature:** The green/purple split. Fuji expired film often has greenish shadows AND purplish highlights simultaneously — a dual color cast that's very recognizable and harder to emulate with simple global white balance.

**"Faded Summer" compatibility:** Lower. The Fuji expired look is less "golden summer" and more "eerie dream." It's beautiful but different — think overcast beach rather than sunny attic.

---

### Fuji Superia 200 / Fujicolor 200

Lower ISO Fuji consumer film.

**Aging pattern:**
- Similar to Superia 400 but slower degradation
- More stable overall — 15-year Superia 200 looks like 8-year Superia 400
- Base fog is lighter, greener
- Color shifts are more subtle
- Still has the purple/green split tone but less pronounced

---

### Fuji Superia 800 / Superia 1600 (discontinued)

High-speed Fuji films. Very fast degradation.

**Aging pattern:**
- Degrades extremely fast — 5 years in heat is equivalent to 15 years for Superia 200
- Heavy base fog, densely green
- Color reproduction often collapses almost entirely
- Grain becomes surreal — looks more like sand than film grain
- Often unusable at 10+ years unless shot with extreme overexposure (+4 stops)

## Agfa / European Consumer Films

### Agfa Vista 200 / Agfacolor 200

Agfa's consumer films (often rebadged as drugstore brands in Europe) use yet another dye coupler chemistry.

**Aging pattern:**
- **Base fog:** Yellow-brown, similar to Kodak but lighter/brighter — more "yellow" than "brown"
- **Color shift:**
  - Overall warm shift similar to Kodak
  - Yellow dominance is even stronger than Kodak
  - Blues degrade to a distinctive pale yellow-blue (odd but recognizable)
  - Greens degrade to bright yellow-olive (brighter than Kodak's brown-olive)
  - Reds hold up slightly better than Kodak
- **Contrast loss:** Significant — Agfa consumer films were lower contrast to begin with
- **Grain:** Moderate. Agfa films have a softer grain structure.
- **Distinctive feature:** The yellowness. Agfa expired film is the most yellow of all common stocks. If Gold 200 is "golden," Agfa Vista is "buttery."

---

### Agfa Portrait / Agfa Optima (discontinued)

Agfa's premium films. Better dye couplers = different aging.

**Aging pattern:**
- More stable than Vista/consumer lines
- Base fog is more neutral — less yellow, more gray
- Color shift is toward pastel — everything desaturates evenly rather than shifting hue dramatically
- Skin tones degrade to a pleasant pale peach (unlike Kodak's Oompa-Loompa orange)
- More "elegant" degradation — preferred by some for portrait work

## Drugstore / Generic / Rebranded Films

A significant portion of "found film" is generic-branded consumer film — actually manufactured by Kodak, Fuji, Agfa, or Ferrania and rebadged. These often use older or cheaper emulsion formulas.

**Common rebrands:** Walgreens, CVS, Rite Aid, Walmart, Tesco, Boots, DM, etc. (mostly 200 or 400 ISO)

**Aging pattern:**
- **Most unpredictable.** Because the underlying manufacturer varies, results range from Gold 200-like to Superia-like to completely strange.
- **Generally worse aging:** These films used cheaper dye couplers and less stable emulsions.
- **Base fog:** Often heavy, muddy, and unattractive — gray-brown rather than golden
- **Color shifts:** Can be extreme and non-linear. Colors don't just shift — they can invert or produce crossover (different color in shadows vs. highlights).
- **Light seal failure:** Generic canisters often had poor light seals that fail with age, producing light leaks in addition to chemical degradation.

**For emulation:** The generic drugstore look is the most "damaged" aesthetic. Good reference for when you want the degradation to be the subject, not just a flavor.

## Slide (Reversal) Films — For Cross-Reference

Slide films (E-6 process) age differently from color negative. While the "Faded Summer" look specifically targets color negative degradation, understanding slide film aging provides useful color reference.

### Kodak Ektachrome (expired 2000–2010)

**Aging pattern:**
- **Fog:** Much less base fog than color negative — slide films have inherently lower fog levels
- **Color shift:** Toward magenta/purple (opposite of color negative's warmth)
- **Contrast:** Increases rather than decreases (D-min rises faster than D-max falls)
- **Distinctive feature:** Purple/magenta color cast, increased contrast, blue shadows. Virtually the opposite of the "Faded Summer" look.

### Kodak Kodachrome (expired, undeveloped)

Kodachrome's unique K-14 process means it ages differently than any other film:
- The dye couplers are in the developer, not the film — so aged Kodachrome is actually more stable (no couplers to degrade)
- However, the latent image deteriorates, and color balance drifts toward cyan/blue
- Undeveloped Kodachrome can no longer be processed in color (K-14 chemicals discontinued in 2010); only black-and-white development is possible

## Specific Degradation Patterns by Storage Condition

### Hot Attic (30-50°C, cyclic)

The archetype for "Faded Summer":
- Strongest base fog (heat accelerates silver reduction)
- Most pronounced color shifts (heat degrades dye couplers)
- Grain clumping is extreme
- Emulsion can partially melt and re-set, creating texture
- Seasonal cycles cause uneven degradation (more in summer, less in winter)

### Hot Car Glovebox (40-70°C, peak cycling)

More extreme than attic:
- Can render film nearly unusable in 5 years
- Dye couplers break down so thoroughly that color reproduction collapses
- Emulsion can literally separate from the film base
- Color shifts go beyond warm to "confused" — multiple competing casts
- Often produces surreal, un-reproducible results

### Basement / Damp Storage

Cooler but humid — different degradation:
- Less color shift (cooler temperature preserves dyes)
- Fungus growth is common — creates permanent physical artifacts on the emulsion
- Base fog is grayer (humidity fog, not thermal fog)
- Film base can become sticky or warped
- Not the "Faded Summer" look — more "horror film"

### Refrigerated / Cold Stored

Frozen or refrigerated film ages VERY slowly:
- 20-year frozen film can look nearly identical to fresh
- Minimal base fog
- Minimal color shift
- The "rule of thumb" that film loses 1 stop per decade only applies to room-temperature or warmer storage

### Room Temperature, Dark Drawer

The baseline aging condition:
- Slow, steady degradation
- Negligible color shift before 10 years
- Light base fog after 10-15 years
- Shootable at box speed for ~10 years, then +1 stop per subsequent decade

## The Overexposure Factor Per Stock

How different stocks respond to the +2 stop overexposure that defines this look:

| Film Stock | +2 Stops Fresh | +2 Stops Expired | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|-------|
| Kodak Gold 200 | Slight highlight bloom, warmer | The signature look: creamy highlights, warm mud shadows, green collapse | The reference standard |
| Kodak Ultramax 400 | Acceptable highlight roll-off | More grain, more magenta shadow fog, more contrast loss | Punchier version of Gold |
| Fuji Superia 400 | Cooler, slightly desaturated | Green/purple split tone, less warm, grainier | Alternative palette |
| Agfa Vista 200 | Yellow-warm pastel | Extremely yellow, very low contrast, soft | Most "romantic" variant |
| Generic 200 | Unpredictable | Maximum chaos — heavy fog, extreme grain, nonlinear color | Most "damaged" variant |

Color negative film handles overexposure gracefully by design (it compresses highlights rather than clipping). This is why +2 stops doesn't ruin the image — the film was engineered to tolerate it. The combination of graceful overexposure tolerance AND chemical degradation creates the specific look.

## Reference Image Characteristics (What to Look For)

When studying real expired film scans on r/analog or Flickr for reference, look for these markers:

### In a Typical Gold 200 Expired +2 Stops Scan:

1. **Black border/rebate area**: Not black — dark amber/brown. If the film edges are true black, it's fresh film.
2. **Sky**: Pale cyan or nearly white, not blue. If the sky is strongly blue, it's either fresh film or someone has color-corrected the scan.
3. **Foliage/grass**: Yellow-brown or olive, not green. This is the most reliable tell.
4. **Shadows**: Brown mud, not gray or black. Details are visible but low-contrast.
5. **Skin**: Orange-tan glow, not natural pink. Looks like a spray tan.
6. **Grain pattern**: Visible in midtones and shadows, clumpy not uniform.
7. **Frame-to-frame variation**: Slight differences in color balance and fog level between consecutive frames.

### In a Typical Superia 400 Expired Scan:

1. **Black border**: Dark green or purple, not brown (major difference from Kodak)
2. **Sky**: Cyan with purple tint, not pale/washed
3. **Foliage**: Desaturated olive-green (not brown!)
4. **Shadows**: Green-purple crossover — shadows lean green, deep shadows lean purple
5. **Skin**: Cooler than Kodak, slightly magenta cast

## Notable Photographers and Projects Using Expired Film

### Community References (r/analog, Flickr)

These are community-known photographers whose work exemplifies the expired film aesthetic:

- **"Found Film" projects**: Many photographers specifically seek out and shoot expired film, documenting the results by stock, age, and storage condition. Search r/analog for "expired Gold 200 results" for dozens of reference images.
- **Flickr groups**: "Expired Film" group has thousands of tagged images organized by film stock — an excellent reference library.
- **Lomography community**: The "Expired" tag on Lomography.com has extensive documentation of degradation patterns.

### Artistic Intent

Photographers who deliberately use expired film cite:
- The unpredictability as a creative constraint
- The nostalgic/emotional quality of the colors
- The material connection to analog processes
- The anti-perfection stance
- The unique palette that cannot be consistently reproduced with fresh film

## Key Takeaways for Emulation

1. **Kodak Gold 200 expired = the reference standard** for "Faded Summer." Warm amber fog, green → brown, heavy grain, golden highlights.

2. **Fuji Superia expired = the alternative palette.** Cooler, greener fog, blue → cyan/purple, finer grain. Useful reference for a different expired look.

3. **Base fog color is the fingerprint.** The color of the lifted blacks (amber for Kodak, green for Fuji, yellow for Agfa) is the most important single parameter for stock-specific emulation.

4. **Green collapse is universal.** All color negative films lose green reproduction when heat-damaged. The degree varies, but the direction (green → yellow/olive/brown) is consistent.

5. **The overexposure is not optional.** It's integral to the look. Without the +2 stop overexposure, you just have underexposed foggy film (muddy and dark). The overexposure pushes through the fog and creates the characteristic creamy highlights.

6. **Scanner / lab correction fights the look.** Most expired film scans you see online have been partially color-corrected by the lab or scanning software. The true, uncorrected expired look is MORE extreme than what you typically see. When emulating, consider going slightly further than reference images suggest.
