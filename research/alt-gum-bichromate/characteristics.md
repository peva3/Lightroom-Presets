# Gum Bichromate — Complete Aesthetic Breakdown

## Core Aesthetic Signature

The gum bichromate process, perfected by Robert Demachy and other pictorialists around 1895, is a hand-coated pigment process using gum arabic, potassium dichromate, and watercolor pigments. Multiple coating/exposure/development cycles can build up layers of different colors, creating uniquely textured, painterly images. It was the quintessential pictorialist process — deliberately un-photographic, soft, and individualistic.

## Historical Context

**Era:** 1895–1920s (pictorialist peak)

Gum arabic + potassium/sodium dichromate + watercolor pigment → coated on paper → UV exposure through negative → dichromate hardens gum in proportion to light → water development washes away unexposed (soft) gum → pigment remains embedded in hardened gum. Multiple layers can be printed in register.

## Visual Characteristics

Painterly, soft, textured surface. Each print is unique due to the hand-coating process. Color palette is limited and muted — earth tones, soft blues, muted greens. Visible brushstroke or coating texture. Soft focus with no sharp edges. The gum arabic creates a slight relief texture.

## Emotional & Artistic Vocabulary

Romantic, painterly, impressionistic. Gum prints reject photographic sharpness in favor of emotional truth. Each print is a handmade object rather than a mechanical reproduction.

## Technical Notes for Digital Emulation

This preset is designed to capture the essential visual character of the gum bichromate process using Lightroom's slider-based toolset. It prioritizes the emotional/aesthetic impact over perfect chemical accuracy, which would require a 3D LUT or DCP camera profile.

### Limitations of Slider-Based Simulation
- True process-specific spectral sensitivity cannot be replicated with sliders
- Chemical stain patterns are simulated through grain and color grading
- Paper/plate texture is approximated through grain settings
